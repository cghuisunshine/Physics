22.1 What Is an Electric Field?

Key ideas

Electric charges exert forces without contact. The electric field is a way to describe how a charge changes the space around it. Another charge placed in that region feels a force because it is inside the field.

Field exists even if no test charge is present Field is a property of space Direction: where a positive charge would accelerate

Important formulas & meaning

Concept preview: E is defined as force per unit positive charge (see 22.1.1).
Worked Example (Concept): Why introduce E instead of only using Coulomb force?
  1. Problem: Force depends on both the source charge(s) and the test charge.
  2. Idea: Separate “space caused by sources” from “response of a test charge.”
  3. Define: E = F / q0. Now E depends only on the source charges and geometry.
  4. Use: Once E is known, any charge q experiences F = qE.

Interpretation: E is like a “force map” created by sources; q tells how strongly an object responds.

22.1.1 Definition of the Electric Field

Key ideas

Definition: The electric field at a point equals the force on a small positive test charge divided by the test charge.

“Small” means it does not disturb the original charge distribution.

Direction: The direction of E is the direction of the force on a positive test charge.

A negative charge feels force opposite the field direction.

Important formulas & their meaning

E = F / q0
Symbol Meaning Units
E Electric field vector at a point N/C
F Electric force on the test charge N
q0 Positive test charge (small) C
Worked Example: Find E from a known force on a test charge

Given: A +2.0 μC test charge experiences a force of 0.060 N to the right.

Find: Electric field E at that point.

  1. Convert charge: 2.0 μC = 2.0 × 10−6 C.
  2. Use definition: E = F/q0.
  3. E = 0.060 / (2.0 × 10−6) = 3.0 × 104 N/C.
  4. Direction is the same as force on + charge: to the right.

Answer: E = 3.0 × 104 N/C to the right.

22.1.2 Electric Field as a Vector Field

Key ideas

Electric fields are vector fields: at every point in space, the field has a magnitude and direction. This is why vector addition is essential when multiple charges contribute.

Magnitude + direction at each point Add using vectors, not just numbers Often use components (x/y) to add

Important formulas & meaning

If E has components: E = (Ex)i + (Ey)j and |E| = √(Ex2 + Ey2)
Worked Example: Add two perpendicular electric field vectors

Given: E1 = 300 N/C east, E2 = 400 N/C north.

Find: Net field magnitude and direction.

  1. Choose axes: east = +x, north = +y.
  2. Ex = 300, Ey = 400 (N/C).
  3. |E| = √(3002 + 4002) = √(90,000 + 160,000) = √250,000 = 500 N/C.
  4. Angle from east: θ = tan−1(Ey/Ex) = tan−1(400/300) ≈ 53° north of east.

Answer: Enet = 500 N/C at ~53° north of east.

22.1.3 Electric Field Lines

Key ideas

Electric field lines are a drawing tool for visualizing electric fields. They are not “real wires” in space—just a way to show direction and relative strength.

Rules/patterns/conditions

Rule Meaning (simple)
Tangent rule E is tangent to the field line at every point.
Density rule Closer lines ⇒ stronger field (larger |E|).
Start/end Lines start on + charges and end on − charges (or infinity).
No crossing Lines never intersect (field can’t have two directions at one point).
Worked Example: Determine field direction near a negative charge

Question: What is the direction of E near an isolated −q charge?

  1. Field direction is defined by force on a positive test charge.
  2. A positive test charge is attracted to a negative source charge.
  3. So E points toward the negative charge at every point.

Answer: Field lines point inward; E is directed toward −q.

Common mistake: Saying “field lines show the path a charge will take.” In general, motion depends on the particle’s initial velocity too. Field lines show instantaneous force direction, not a guaranteed trajectory.

22.2 Electric Field Due to a Point Charge

Key ideas

A point charge creates a radial electric field. The magnitude falls off as 1/r2. Direction is outward for +q and inward for −q.

Important formulas & their meaning

E = (1 / (4π ε0)) · (q / r2) · r
Magnitude: E = (1 / (4π ε0)) · (|q| / r2)

Here r is a unit vector pointing from the source charge to the field point (radially outward).

Worked Example: Field magnitude at a distance from a point charge

Given: q = +3.0 nC, r = 0.20 m. Use k = 1/(4π ε0) ≈ 8.99×109 N·m2/C2.

Find: E at that distance (magnitude + direction).

  1. Convert: 3.0 nC = 3.0 × 10−9 C.
  2. E = k|q|/r2 = (8.99×109)(3.0×10−9)/(0.20)2
  3. Compute numerator: 8.99×109 × 3.0×10−9 = 26.97
  4. Denominator: (0.20)2 = 0.040
  5. E = 26.97 / 0.040 ≈ 6.74×102 N/C
  6. Direction: away from +q (radially outward)

Answer: E ≈ 6.7×102 N/C, pointing away from the charge.

22.2.1 Superposition of Electric Fields

Key ideas

When multiple charges are present, the net electric field is the vector sum of the fields produced by each charge separately.

Important formulas & their meaning

Enet = E1 + E2 + E3 + … (vector addition)
Do not add magnitudes unless the directions are the same. Use components if directions differ.
Worked Example: Net field at midpoint between +q and +q

Setup: Two identical +5.0 nC charges are separated by 0.40 m. Find E at the midpoint.

  1. Midpoint is 0.20 m from each charge.
  2. Each charge produces field magnitude E = kq/r2 at midpoint.
  3. But directions are opposite: each field points away from its + charge.
  4. At the midpoint, one field points right, the other left (equal magnitude).
  5. Vector sum cancels: Enet = 0.

Answer: Enet = 0 N/C at the midpoint (symmetry cancellation).

Worked Example: Net field at midpoint between +q and −q (dipole-like)

Setup: +5.0 nC and −5.0 nC separated by 0.40 m. Find E at midpoint.

  1. Midpoint is 0.20 m from each charge.
  2. Field from +q points away from +q (toward the −q side).
  3. Field from −q points toward −q (also toward the −q side).
  4. Directions match, so magnitudes add: Enet = 2(kq/r2).

Answer: Nonzero field pointing from + toward − at the midpoint.

22.3 Electric Field of an Electric Dipole

Key ideas

Dipole: two equal and opposite charges separated by distance d.

Many molecules behave like dipoles (polar molecules).

Dipole moment: measures “strength” of the dipole.

p = q d

Direction is from − to +.

Important formulas & their meaning

Dipole axis (far away, z ≫ d): E ≈ (1 / (2π ε0)) · (p / z3)
Big idea: dipole fields decrease as 1/z3 (faster than a point charge’s 1/r2).
Worked Example: Compare how fast fields drop off (point charge vs dipole)

Question: If you double the distance from a source, how does E change?

  1. Point charge: E ∝ 1/r2. Doubling r ⇒ E becomes (1/22)=1/4 as large.
  2. Dipole (far field): E ∝ 1/r3. Doubling r ⇒ E becomes (1/23)=1/8 as large.

Answer: Dipole fields weaken faster with distance than point-charge fields.

22.4 Electric Field of a Continuous Charge Distribution

Key ideas

Real objects are not point charges. For rods, rings, disks, etc., treat the charge as many tiny pieces dq, compute dE from each piece, then add them up using symmetry and integration.

Rules/patterns/conditions

  1. Break the object into elements dq.
  2. Write dq using a charge density (λ, σ, ρ) and a small length/area/volume element.
  3. Write dE using point-charge formula: dE = k dq / r2 (vector form).
  4. Use symmetry to cancel components (often sideways components cancel).
  5. Integrate over the entire object.

Important formulas & meaning

Density type Symbol Definition Units
Linear λ dq = λ dl C/m
Surface σ dq = σ dA C/m2
Volume ρ dq = ρ dV C/m3
Worked Example (Method): Uniformly charged rod — what cancels by symmetry?

Setup: A uniformly charged rod lies on the x-axis, and we want E at a point on the perpendicular bisector.

  1. Pick a small element dq on the left and a matching dq on the right (same distance to the point).
  2. Their horizontal components point in opposite directions ⇒ cancel.
  3. Their vertical components point the same way ⇒ add.
  4. So you integrate only the component that survives (usually the perpendicular direction).

Takeaway: symmetry can reduce a 2D/3D vector addition problem into a 1D integral.

22.4.1 Charged Ring (Field on the axis)

Key ideas

For a uniformly charged ring, sideways components cancel by symmetry. Only the axial component survives.

Important formulas & their meaning

On the axis: E = (q z) / (4π ε0 (z2 + R2)3/2)
Symbol Meaning
qTotal charge on ring
RRing radius
zDistance from center along axis
Worked Example: Field at the center of a charged ring

Given: Any charged ring (total charge q), find E at z = 0 (the center).

  1. Use the axis formula: E = (q z) / (4π ε0 (z2 + R2)3/2).
  2. At center, z = 0 ⇒ numerator qz = 0.
  3. So E = 0.

Answer: E = 0 at the exact center (perfect symmetry).

22.5 Electric Field of a Charged Disk (Field on the axis)

Key ideas

A uniformly charged disk can be treated as many rings. Integrating ring contributions gives the axial field.

Important formulas & their meaning

E = (σ / (2 ε0)) · (1 − z / √(z2 + R2))

σ is surface charge density; R is disk radius; z is distance along axis from disk center.

Worked Example: Infinite sheet limit (why E becomes constant)

Idea: Let the disk radius R become extremely large compared with z (R ≫ z).

  1. If R ≫ z, then √(z2 + R2) ≈ R.
  2. Then z/√(z2+R2) ≈ z/R ≈ 0.
  3. So E ≈ (σ/(2 ε0))(1 − 0) = σ/(2 ε0).

Result: For an (effectively) infinite charged sheet, E is constant: E = σ/(2 ε0).

Concept check: “Constant field” does not mean “no field.” It means the magnitude does not change with distance from the sheet (ideal infinite model).

22.6 A Charge in an Electric Field

Key ideas

Once you know E at a point, the force on a charge q placed there is immediate. A positive charge accelerates along E; a negative charge accelerates opposite E.

Important formulas & their meaning

F = q E
Charge signForce direction
q > 0Same direction as E
q < 0Opposite direction to E
Worked Example: Force on an electron in a known field

Given: Uniform field E = 2.0×104 N/C to the right. Electron charge q = −1.60×10−19 C.

Find: Force on the electron (magnitude + direction).

  1. Use F = qE.
  2. Magnitude: |F| = |q|E = (1.60×10−19)(2.0×104) = 3.2×10−15 N.
  3. Direction: q is negative ⇒ force is opposite E ⇒ to the left.

Answer: F = 3.2×10−15 N to the left.

22.7 Electric Dipole in an External Electric Field

Key ideas

A dipole in a uniform external field experiences a torque that tends to align the dipole with the field. Its potential energy depends on its orientation angle.

Important formulas & their meaning

τ = p × E   ⇒   τ = pE sinθ
U = −p · E   ⇒   U = −pE cosθ
Worked Example: Maximum torque condition

Question: At what angle θ is the torque on a dipole maximum?

  1. Torque magnitude: τ = pE sinθ.
  2. sinθ is maximum at 1 when θ = 90°.
  3. So τmax = pE occurs when dipole is perpendicular to the field.

Answer: Maximum torque at θ = 90°; τmax = pE.

Worked Example: Lowest potential energy orientation

Question: Which orientation has minimum U?

  1. U = −pE cosθ.
  2. cosθ is maximum at 1 when θ = 0°.
  3. Thus U is most negative (minimum) when dipole aligns with the field.

Answer: Minimum energy at θ = 0° (dipole aligned with E).

Study tip: For electric field problems, always decide (1) direction first, then (2) magnitude, and finally (3) vector addition (components) if multiple sources exist.
