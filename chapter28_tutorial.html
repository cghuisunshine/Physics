28.1 Magnetic Fields and the Definition of B

Key ideas

Unlike electric fields, magnetic fields are not defined by forces on a stationary test charge. Instead, we define B through the force on a moving charge.

B is a vector field Force depends on velocity Force is perpendicular to v and B

28.1.1 Magnetic Force on a Moving Charge

Core law

FB = q(v × B)
FB = |q| vB sinϕ
Symbol Meaning Unit
B Magnetic field T (tesla)
q Charge (sign included) C
v Velocity m/s
Worked Example: When is the magnetic force zero?
  1. From FB = |q|vB sinϕ, we need sinϕ = 0.
  2. That means ϕ = 0° or 180°: velocity parallel or antiparallel to B.

Answer: No magnetic deflection if the particle moves along field lines.

28.1.2 Direction (Right-Hand Rule)

Key ideas

The cross product direction comes from the right-hand rule: curl fingers from v to B, thumb gives v × B. Then multiply by q: negative charges reverse the force direction.

Worked Example: Force direction for a positive charge

Setup: v points east, B points up. Find direction of FB for q > 0.

  1. Compute v × B: east × up = south (using ENU right-hand coordinate sense).
  2. q > 0 ⇒ FB points south.

Answer: South for a positive charge; north for a negative charge.

28.1.3 Work and Energy

Key idea

Magnetic force does no work on a moving charge because it is always perpendicular to the velocity. It can change the direction of motion but not the speed (kinetic energy).

This is why uniform magnetic fields produce circular/helical paths: they bend trajectories without changing kinetic energy.

28.2 Crossed Fields: Discovery of the Electron

Key ideas

When uniform electric and magnetic fields are perpendicular, a charged particle can be made to travel straight if the electric and magnetic forces cancel. This “crossed-fields” setup was central to early measurements of electron properties.

28.2.1 Velocity Selector

Key result

If |q|E = |q|vB, then v = E/B
Worked Example: Selecting a speed

Given: E = 3.0 × 104 V/m and B = 0.20 T.

  1. v = E/B = (3.0×104)/(0.20) = 1.5×105 m/s.

Answer: v = 1.5 × 105 m/s.

28.2.2 Why Crossed Fields Are Powerful

Key ideas

Electric deflection depends on E and q, magnetic deflection depends on v and B. Using both lets you separate unknowns like v, q, and m (historically enabling m/|q| measurements).

28.3 Crossed Fields: The Hall Effect

Key ideas

A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences sideways magnetic forces on its charge carriers. Charges build up on the sides until an internal electric field forms and balances the magnetic force, creating a measurable Hall voltage.

28.3.1 Hall Voltage and Carrier Density

Useful relations

V = Ed
At balance: E = vB   ⇒   V = vBd
Carrier density (strip thickness l):   n = Bi/(Vle)
Worked Example: Hall voltage from motion

Given: A conductor moves at v = 4.0 m/s through B = 0.050 T. Width d = 1.5 cm.

  1. V = vBd = (4.0)(0.050)(0.015) = 0.0030 V.

Answer: 3.0 mV.

28.4 A Circulating Charged Particle

Key ideas

In a uniform magnetic field, a charged particle moves in a circle if v ⟂ B. If there is also a component of velocity parallel to B, the path becomes a helix.

Circular: v ⟂ B Straight line: v ∥ B Helix: v has both components

28.4.1 Circular Motion Basics

Key results

r = mv/(|q|B)
ω = |q|B/m   ⇒   T = 2π/ω = 2πm/(|q|B)
Worked Example: Radius of an electron path

Given: Electron speed v = 2.0 × 107 m/s in B = 0.020 T. Use m = 9.11×10−31 kg, |q| = e.

  1. r = mv/(eB) ≈ (9.11×10−31)(2.0×107)/[(1.60×10−19)(0.020)].
  2. r ≈ 5.7 × 10−3 m.

Answer: r ≈ 5.7 mm.

28.4.2 Helical Motion

Key ideas

Decompose velocity into components: v⊥ (perpendicular) creates circular motion, v∥ (parallel) causes forward motion along field lines. The helical pitch is the distance advanced in one period.

Useful relations

r = mv⊥/(|q|B)
pitch p = v∥T

28.5 Cyclotrons and Synchrotrons

Key ideas

Accelerators use electric fields to increase kinetic energy and magnetic fields to bend particle paths so the particles pass through the accelerating region repeatedly.

28.5.1 Cyclotron Frequency (Nonrelativistic)

Key result

f = ω/(2π) = |q|B/(2πm)

In the ideal cyclotron, this frequency does not depend on speed (as long as v ≪ c so m is effectively constant).

28.5.2 Why Synchrotrons Are Needed

Key idea

At very high speeds, relativistic effects increase the effective inertia. The particle’s revolution frequency shifts, so a fixed RF accelerating frequency no longer stays in resonance; synchrotrons adjust fields/timing to keep acceleration effective.

28.6 Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

Key ideas

A magnetic field exerts a force on the moving charges in a wire. By Newton’s third law, the wire experiences an equal and opposite force, producing macroscopic deflection and motor action.

28.6.1 Force on a Straight Segment

Key laws

FB = i(L × B)
dFB = i(dL × B)
Worked Example: Magnitude and direction of wire force

Given: A straight wire segment of length L = 0.20 m carries i = 5.0 A. B = 0.30 T is perpendicular to the wire.

  1. Magnitude: F = iLB = (5.0)(0.20)(0.30) = 0.30 N.
  2. Direction: use right-hand rule on L × B (current direction sets L).

Answer: 0.30 N, perpendicular to both the current direction and the field.

28.6.2 Curved Wires and Nonuniform Fields

Key idea

When the wire is curved or B varies with position, compute the force by integrating: dFB = i(dL × B) along the wire.

28.7 Torque on a Current Loop

Key ideas

In a uniform magnetic field, a loop can have zero net force but a nonzero net torque that tends to rotate the loop (motor principle). The torque is maximum when the loop’s normal is perpendicular to B.

28.7.1 Torque Magnitude

Result

τ = NiAB sinθ
Worked Example: Maximum torque and orientation

Question: When is τ maximum?

  1. τ = NiAB sinθ is maximum when sinθ = 1.
  2. So θ = 90° (normal to loop is perpendicular to B).

Answer: Maximum torque at θ = 90°.

28.8 The Magnetic Dipole Moment

Key ideas

A current loop behaves like a magnetic dipole. Its dipole moment vector μ points along the loop’s normal (right-hand rule) and determines torque and energy in an external field.

28.8.1 Dipole Moment and Orientation Energy

Key relations

μ has magnitude μ = NiA
τ = μ × B
U(θ) = −μ · B = −μB cosθ
Worked Example: Lowest-energy orientation

Question: For fixed μ and B, what θ minimizes U?

  1. U = −μB cosθ is minimum when cosθ is maximum.
  2. cosθ is maximum at 1 when θ = 0°.

Answer: Minimum energy when the dipole aligns with the field (θ = 0°).

Study tip: Separate “direction” problems (right-hand rules) from “magnitude” problems (sinϕ factors, circular motion). For many mistakes, the culprit is swapping v × B into B × v.
