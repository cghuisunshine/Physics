38.1 The Photon, the Quantum of Light

Key ideas

Light energy is delivered in discrete packets called photons. A photon’s energy depends on frequency: higher frequency means higher energy per photon.

Energy is quantized Higher f → higher E Intensity ↔ photon rate

Photon energy

E = hf = hc/λ

h is Planck’s constant, c is the speed of light.

38.1.1 Photon Energy and Intensity

Photon rate from power

Ṅ = P/(hf)
Worked Example: Photons per second in a laser beam

Given: Power P = 2.0 mW, wavelength λ = 633 nm.

  1. Photon energy: E = hc/λ = (6.63×10−34)(3.00×108)/(6.33×10−7) = 3.14×10−19 J.
  2. Ṅ = P/E = (2.0×10−3)/(3.14×10−19) = 6.4×1015 s−1.

Answer: About 6×1015 photons per second.

38.2 The Photoelectric Effect

Key ideas

Light shining on a metal can eject electrons if photon energy is high enough. The key experimental feature is a cutoff frequency: below it, no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity.

38.2.1 Einstein’s Photoelectric Equation

Energy accounting

hf = Kmax + Φ
Kmax = eVstop

Φ (work function) is the minimum energy needed for an electron to escape. At cutoff, hf0 = Φ.

Worked Example: Stopping potential

Given: Light frequency f = 9.0×1014 Hz, work function Φ = 2.3 eV.

  1. Photon energy: hf = (4.14×10−15 eV·s)(9.0×1014 s−1) = 3.73 eV.
  2. Kmax = hf − Φ = 3.73 − 2.3 = 1.43 eV.
  3. Vstop = Kmax/e = 1.43 V.

Answer: Vstop ≈ 1.4 V.

38.2.2 Stopping Potential vs Frequency

Linear relationship

Vstop = (h/e)f − Φ/e

A plot of Vstop vs f is a straight line. The slope is h/e, and the frequency intercept gives the cutoff frequency f0 = Φ/h.

38.3 Photons, Momentum, Compton Scattering

Key ideas

Photons carry momentum even though they have no rest mass. In Compton scattering, an x-ray photon collides with an electron; the scattered photon has a longer wavelength, showing particle-like momentum transfer.

38.3.1 Photon Momentum

Momentum–wavelength relation

p = E/c = hf/c = h/λ
Worked Example: Photon momentum

Given: Green light λ = 532 nm.

  1. p = h/λ = (6.63×10−34)/(5.32×10−7) = 1.25×10−27 kg·m/s.

Answer: p ≈ 1.25×10−27 kg·m/s.

38.3.2 Compton Shift

Wavelength shift

Δλ = λ′ − λ = (h/mec)(1 − cosθ)
λC = h/(mec) = 2.43 pm
Worked Example: Maximal Compton shift

Given: Backscatter θ = 180°.

  1. 1 − cosθ = 1 − (−1) = 2.
  2. Δλ = 2λC = 4.86 pm.

Answer: Maximum shift is 4.86 pm.

38.4 The Birth of Quantum Physics

Key ideas

Several phenomena forced the abandonment of purely classical physics: blackbody radiation, the photoelectric effect, atomic spectra, and the stability/structure of atoms. The common theme is quantization and wave–particle duality.

38.4.1 Quantization Ideas

What to remember

  1. Energy exchange with radiation can occur in discrete quanta (photons).
  2. Matter (electrons) also shows wave behavior (diffraction/interference).
  3. Quantum theory predicts probabilities, not deterministic trajectories.

38.5 Electrons and Matter Waves

Key ideas

de Broglie proposed that matter has wave character: a particle with momentum p has a wavelength λ. This is confirmed by electron diffraction from crystals.

Wave–particle duality λ depends on momentum Diffraction is the evidence

de Broglie relation

λ = h/p

For nonrelativistic particles, p = mv.

38.5.1 de Broglie Wavelength (Electron Accelerated Through V)

Nonrelativistic electron approximation

K = eV = p2/(2me)   ⇒   λ = h/√(2meeV)
Worked Example: Electron wavelength at 150 V

Given: V = 150 V.

  1. λ = h/√(2meeV) = (6.63×10−34)/√(2·9.11×10−31·1.60×10−19·150).
  2. λ ≈ 1.0×10−10 m = 0.10 nm.

Answer: λ ≈ 0.10 nm (atomic-scale).

38.5.2 Electron Diffraction (Bragg Picture)

Crystal as a grating

2d sinθ = mλ

Electron diffraction peaks occur at angles where reflections from successive crystal planes are in phase, exactly like x-ray diffraction.

38.6 Schrödinger’s Equation

Key ideas

Quantum mechanics describes a particle by a complex wavefunction ψ. The measurable prediction is probability: |ψ|2 gives the probability density (where the particle is likely to be found).

38.6.1 Wavefunction Meaning

Probability density

P(x) = |ψ(x)|2

Unlike classical waves, ψ is not directly a physical displacement; it is a probability amplitude. Normalization ensures total probability is 1.

38.6.2 Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation (1D)

Standard form

−(ħ2/2m) d2ψ/dx2 + U(x)ψ = Eψ

For regions where U is constant, solutions are sinusoidal if E > U (traveling/standing waves) and exponential if E < U (decaying waves).

38.7 Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle

Key idea

You cannot simultaneously know a particle’s position and momentum with arbitrary precision. This is not measurement “sloppiness” but a fundamental wave property: a localized wave packet requires a spread of wavelengths (momenta).

38.7.1 Uncertainty Relations

Core form

ΔxΔp ≥ ħ/2
ΔEΔt ≥ ħ/2
Worked Example: Momentum uncertainty from localization

Given: An electron is localized to Δx = 0.10 nm.

  1. Δp ≥ ħ/(2Δx) = (1.05×10−34)/(2·1.0×10−10) = 5.3×10−25 kg·m/s.

Answer: Δp is at least ~5×10−25 kg·m/s.

38.8 Reflection from a Potential Step

Key idea

Classically, if E > U, a particle always passes a step. Quantum mechanically, even when E > U, partial reflection can occur because wave amplitudes must match smoothly at the boundary.

38.8.1 Classical vs Quantum Reflection

Qualitative takeaway

Treat the particle as a wave: at a step, part of the wave is reflected and part transmitted, like a string wave at a boundary. Probabilities come from the squared amplitudes.

38.9 Tunneling Through a Potential Barrier

Key idea

If E < U inside a barrier, the wavefunction decays exponentially but can still have a nonzero amplitude on the far side. That leads to a nonzero probability of transmission: tunneling.

38.9.1 Tunneling Probability Idea

Exponential dependence (qualitative)

T decreases rapidly with barrier height and width

Tunneling is crucial in nuclear decay (alpha decay), scanning tunneling microscopes, and modern electronics (e.g., tunnel diodes).

Study tip: keep three “bridges” straight: photons (E = hf, p = h/λ), matter waves (λ = h/p), and quantum prediction (probability from |ψ|2). Most problems are careful unit conversion plus choosing the right bridge.
