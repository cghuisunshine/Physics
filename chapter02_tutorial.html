2.1 Position, Displacement, and Average Velocity

Position x is a coordinate; displacement is Δx = xf − xi. Average velocity uses displacement, not distance.

v̄ = Δx/Δt

2.2 Instantaneous Velocity and Speed

Instantaneous velocity is the slope of the x(t) graph. Speed is the magnitude of velocity (nonnegative).

v = dx/dt

2.3 Acceleration

Acceleration measures how velocity changes. It can be negative even when speed is increasing (depending on sign convention).

ā = Δv/Δt
a = dv/dt = d2x/dt2

2.4 Constant Acceleration

With constant acceleration, kinematics reduces to a small set of equations. Pick the one that uses the variables you know.

v = v0 + at
x − x0 = v0t + (1/2)at2
v2 = v02 + 2a(x − x0)

2.5 Free-Fall Acceleration

Near Earth (ignoring air resistance), free-fall acceleration is approximately constant downward: a = −g. Then vertical motion is just constant-acceleration kinematics.

g ≈ 9.80 m/s2

2.6 Graphical Integration in Motion Analysis

The area under a v(t) curve gives displacement; the slope of v(t) gives acceleration. For a(t), area gives Δv.

Δx = ∫ v(t) dt
Δv = ∫ a(t) dt
Graph tip

Break the curve into simple shapes and compute signed areas. “Below axis” counts negative.

Study tip: write your sign convention first (which direction is +). Most mistakes in 1D kinematics are sign errors, not algebra errors.
