25.1 Capacitance

Key ideas

A capacitor is two conductors separated so charge cannot flow across the gap. When charged, the plates carry +q and −q. The capacitance C tells how much charge is stored per potential difference.

C depends on geometry + material C does not depend on q or V (for linear dielectrics) Unit: 1 F = 1 C/V

Core definition

q = CV    (so C = q/V)
Worked Example: Quick units + scale

Question: If C = 2.2 μF and V = 9.0 V, what is q?

  1. q = CV = (2.2 × 10−6 F)(9.0 V) = 19.8 × 10−6 C.
  2. q = 19.8 μC.

Answer: q ≈ 2.0 × 10−5 C.

25.1.1 Charging with a Battery

Key ideas

Closing the switch lets electrons shift through the wires; equal and opposite charges build up on the plates.

Charging stops when the capacitor’s potential difference matches the battery voltage (no further net drive).

Worked Example (Concept): Does charge “cross the gap”?
  1. In the ideal capacitor model, no conduction charge crosses the dielectric/air gap.
  2. Charge rearranges on each plate via the external circuit; the gap stores energy in the electric field.
  3. The net charge of the capacitor is still zero: +q on one plate, −q on the other.

25.2 Calculating the Capacitance

Key ideas

Recipe: assume charge ±q on conductors → find E from Gauss’ law → find V via V = −∫ E · ds → compute C = q/V.

25.2.1 Parallel-Plate Capacitor

Result

C = ε0A / d

Bigger area A → bigger C. Larger separation d → smaller C. This is why real capacitors use large effective area (rolled foils) and tiny gaps (thin dielectrics).

25.2.2 Cylindrical and Spherical Capacitors

Results

Coaxial cylinders (length L, radii a<b):   C = 2π ε0 L / ln(b/a)
Concentric spheres (radii a<b):   C = 4π ε0 (ab)/(b − a)
Worked Example: Trend check for cylinders

Question: If the outer radius b increases while a and L stay fixed, what happens to C?

  1. C ∝ 1/ln(b/a).
  2. Increasing b increases ln(b/a).
  3. So C decreases (slowly) as b grows.

Answer: C decreases as b increases (for fixed a and L).

25.2.3 Isolated Sphere

Result

C = 4π ε0 R

This “capacitance” is relative to infinity. It shows why isolated objects can store charge even without a second explicit plate.

25.3 Capacitors in Parallel and in Series

Key idea

Learn the two wiring patterns by what they “share”: parallel shares the same V; series shares the same charge q. From that, derive Ceq.

25.3.1 Parallel Rules

Facts

Parallel: V is the same on each capacitor
Ceq = C1 + C2 + …
qtotal = q1 + q2 + …
Worked Example: Two capacitors in parallel

Given: C1 = 2.0 μF, C2 = 3.0 μF across V = 12 V.

  1. Ceq = 5.0 μF.
  2. qtotal = CeqV = (5.0 μF)(12 V) = 60 μC.
  3. Individual charges: q1 = C1V = 24 μC, q2 = 36 μC.

Answer: Ceq = 5.0 μF; q1 = 24 μC; q2 = 36 μC.

25.3.2 Series Rules

Facts

Series: q is the same on each capacitor
1/Ceq = 1/C1 + 1/C2 + …
Vtotal = V1 + V2 + …
Worked Example: Two capacitors in series

Given: C1 = 2.0 μF, C2 = 3.0 μF across V = 12 V.

  1. 1/Ceq = 1/2.0 + 1/3.0 = 5/6 (μF)−1 ⇒ Ceq = 1.2 μF.
  2. Common charge: q = CeqV = (1.2 μF)(12 V) = 14.4 μC.
  3. Voltages: V1 = q/C1 = 7.2 V; V2 = q/C2 = 4.8 V.

Answer: Ceq = 1.2 μF; q = 14.4 μC; V splits as 7.2 V and 4.8 V.

25.3.3 Mixed Networks Strategy

Method

  1. Collapse obvious parallel groups (same two nodes).
  2. Collapse obvious series chains (single path, same charge).
  3. Repeat until one Ceq remains; find q or V using the battery (if present).
  4. Reverse the reductions to recover individual q’s and V’s (parallel: same V; series: same q).

25.4 Energy Stored in an Electric Field

Key ideas

Charging a capacitor requires work. That work becomes electric potential energy, stored in the field in the space between conductors.

25.4.1 Energy Formulas

Equivalent expressions

U = q2/(2C) = (1/2)CV2 = (1/2)qV
Worked Example: Compute stored energy

Given: C = 10 μF, V = 12 V.

  1. U = (1/2)CV2 = 0.5(10×10−6)(122) J.
  2. U = 7.2 × 10−4 J.

Answer: U = 0.72 mJ.

25.4.2 Energy Density

Key idea

The energy is not “in the plates”; it is in the field region. Energy per unit volume (energy density) depends on field strength.

Result

u = (1/2) ε0 E2

Strong E means high u, which is why high-voltage equipment must manage breakdown and sparking.

25.5 Capacitor with a Dielectric

Key ideas

A dielectric is an insulator. In an external field, its molecules polarize, creating induced (bound) charges that reduce the net field inside the material. The net effect is a larger capacitance.

C increases by κ Field inside dielectric is reduced κ > 1 (dielectric constant)

25.5.1 What Changes (C, V, E, U)

Core replacement

If fully filled:   C = κC0    (equivalently, replace ε0 → κ ε0)

Two common scenarios

Battery connected (V fixed): C increases ⇒ q = CV increases; E ≈ V/d stays set by V and geometry.

Battery disconnected (q fixed): C increases ⇒ V = q/C decreases; E decreases.

Worked Example: Insert dielectric with battery disconnected

Given: Initially C0 = 4.0 μF charged to V0 = 100 V, then disconnected. Insert dielectric κ = 5.

  1. Initial charge: q = C0V0 = (4.0 μF)(100 V) = 400 μC.
  2. New capacitance: C = κC0 = 20 μF.
  3. New voltage: V = q/C = 400 μC / 20 μF = 20 V.

Answer: Voltage drops from 100 V to 20 V when κ = 5 (q fixed).

25.6 Dielectrics and Gauss’ Law

Key ideas

With dielectrics, you often track two charges: free charge on conductors and induced (bound) charge on dielectric surfaces. The induced charge reduces the net field in the dielectric.

25.6.1 Free vs. Induced Charge

Key ideas

Free charge: resides on conductors; can move through the circuit when potentials change.

Induced (bound) charge: appears on dielectric surfaces due to polarization; does not flow through wires.

25.6.2 Field Inside a Uniform Dielectric

Parallel-plate intuition

If a uniform dielectric completely fills the gap and κ is constant, the field magnitude inside is reduced by κ for the same free charge.

Useful results (fully filled, uniform)

No dielectric:   E0 = q/(ε0A)
With dielectric:   E = q/(κ ε0A) = E0/κ

If the dielectric only partially fills the gap, you may have multiple regions with different E and must match boundary conditions (often treat as series “slabs”).

Study tip: For capacitor circuits, decide first whether the network is effectively parallel (same V) or series (same q), then use q = CV. For dielectrics, always ask: is V fixed (battery connected) or is q fixed (isolated capacitor)?
