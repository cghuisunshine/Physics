37.1 Simultaneity and Time Dilation

Key ideas

Special relativity applies to inertial frames (constant relative velocity). Observers in different inertial frames can disagree about whether separated events are simultaneous, and they measure different time intervals between the same pair of events.

No absolute simultaneity Proper time is special γ sets the scale

Speed and factors

β = v/c     γ = 1/√(1 − β2)

As v → c, γ grows without bound.

37.1.1 Postulates and the Lorentz Factor

Two postulates (special relativity)

  1. All inertial observers agree on the laws of physics.
  2. The speed of light in vacuum has the same value c in all inertial frames, regardless of source/observer motion.

These postulates force spacetime coordinates (x, t) to mix between frames via Lorentz transformations rather than Galilean transformations.

37.1.2 Proper Time and Time Dilation

Time dilation

Δt = γΔt0

Δt0 (proper time) is measured in the frame where the two events occur at the same place (one clock). Any frame moving relative to that frame measures a larger interval Δt.

Worked Example: Muon lifetime (time dilation)

Given: Proper lifetime Δt0 = 2.2 μs, speed v = 0.98c.

  1. β = 0.98 ⇒ γ = 1/√(1 − 0.982) = 1/√(0.0396) = 5.03.
  2. Δt = γΔt0 = 5.03(2.2 μs) = 11.1 μs.

Answer: Observed lifetime is about 11 μs.

37.2 The Relativity of Length

Key idea

A moving object is measured to be shorter along the direction of motion. The key is simultaneity: a length measurement in a frame requires the positions of the endpoints at the same time in that frame.

37.2.1 Length Contraction

Contraction formula

L = L0/γ

L0 is the proper length (measured in the object’s rest frame). Only lengths parallel to the motion contract.

Worked Example: Contracted length

Given: Proper length L0 = 100 m, speed v = 0.80c.

  1. γ = 1/√(1 − 0.802) = 1/√(0.36) = 1.667.
  2. L = 100/1.667 = 60 m.

Answer: L ≈ 60 m.

37.3 The Lorentz Transformation

Key ideas

Lorentz transformations relate the coordinates of a single event measured in two inertial frames in relative motion. If S′ moves at +v along x relative to S, then x and t mix between frames.

Setup

Assume origins coincide at t = t′ = 0 and motion is along the shared x/x′ axis.

37.3.1 x–t Transforms (1D)

Lorentz transformation

x′ = γ(x − vt)
t′ = γ(t − vx/c2)
y′ = y     z′ = z
Worked Example: Transform an event

Given: In frame S, an event occurs at x = 300 m and t = 2.0 μs. Frame S′ moves at v = 0.60c.

  1. γ = 1/√(1 − 0.602) = 1/0.8 = 1.25.
  2. x′ = γ(x − vt) = 1.25(300 − 0.60c·2.0 μs).
  3. c·2.0 μs = (3.00×108)(2.0×10−6) = 600 m ⇒ vt = 0.60(600) = 360 m.
  4. x′ = 1.25(300 − 360) = −75 m.
  5. t′ = γ(t − vx/c2) = 1.25(2.0 μs − 0.60·300/c).
  6. 300/c = 1.0 μs ⇒ t′ = 1.25(2.0 − 0.60) μs = 1.75 μs.

Answer: x′ = −75 m, t′ = 1.75 μs.

37.3.2 Spacetime Interval Idea

Invariant (1D form)

s2 = (cΔt)2 − (Δx)2

Different frames disagree about Δt and Δx for two events, but they agree on s2. This is the “geometry” behind proper time and proper length.

37.4 The Relativity of Velocities

Key idea

Velocities don’t add by simple subtraction/addition when speeds are high. Relativistic addition guarantees that if each speed is less than c, the resulting speed is also less than c.

37.4.1 Velocity Addition (1D)

Formula

u = (u′ + v)/(1 + u′v/c2)
Worked Example: Two high speeds

Given: A ship moves at v = 0.80c relative to Earth. In the ship frame, a probe moves forward at u′ = 0.60c.

  1. u = (0.60c + 0.80c)/(1 + 0.60·0.80) = 1.40c/1.48.
  2. u = 0.946c.

Answer: The probe speed relative to Earth is 0.946c (not 1.40c).

37.5 Doppler Effect for Light

Key ideas

For light, the Doppler shift must be relativistic. Motion toward the source gives a higher observed frequency (blueshift); motion away gives a lower observed frequency (redshift).

37.5.1 Frequency and Wavelength Shift (Along the Line of Sight)

Frequency form

Approach: f′ = f √((1 + β)/(1 − β))
Recede: f′ = f √((1 − β)/(1 + β))

Wavelength form (inverse)

Approach: λ′ = λ √((1 − β)/(1 + β))
Recede: λ′ = λ √((1 + β)/(1 − β))
Worked Example: Redshift from recession

Given: A galaxy recedes at v = 0.10c. A spectral line has λ = 500 nm in the rest frame.

  1. β = 0.10. For recession: λ′ = λ √((1 + β)/(1 − β)) = 500 √(1.10/0.90) nm.
  2. √(1.222...) = 1.105 ⇒ λ′ ≈ 553 nm.

Answer: Observed wavelength is about 553 nm (redshift).

37.6 Momentum and Energy

Key ideas

To keep conservation laws valid in all inertial frames, momentum and energy must be redefined at high speeds. The classical forms p = mv and K = ½mv2 are recovered when v ≪ c.

Core definitions

p = γmv
E = γmc2     K = (γ − 1)mc2

37.6.1 Relativistic Momentum and Kinetic Energy

Worked Example: Speed from kinetic energy (electron)

Given: Electron kinetic energy K = 2.0 MeV. (Electron rest energy m c2 = 0.511 MeV.)

  1. γ = 1 + K/(mc2) = 1 + 2.0/0.511 = 4.915.
  2. β = √(1 − 1/γ2) = √(1 − 1/4.9152) = 0.979.
  3. v = βc = 0.979c.

Answer: v ≈ 0.98c.

37.6.2 Energy–Momentum Relation

Invariant relationship

E2 = (pc)2 + (mc2)2

For massless particles (m = 0), this gives E = pc (photons). For slow speeds, it reduces to E ≈ mc2 + ½mv2.

Study tip: pick the “best” frame first. If events happen at one place in some frame, that frame gives proper time. If a length is at rest in some frame, that frame gives proper length. Then use γ and (if needed) Lorentz transformations to move to other frames.
