24.1 Electric Potential

Key ideas

The electric force is conservative, so we can describe it using potential energy U and electric potential V (a scalar). In many problems, working with V is easier than working with E because potentials add algebraically.

V is scalar (no direction) ΔV depends only on endpoints Use energy: ΔU = qΔV

Important formulas & meaning

V = U / q
ΔU = qΔV = q(Vf − Vi)
Worked Example (Concept): Why is V useful if we already have E?
  1. Field method: Find E (vector), then force F = qE, then use Newton + kinematics/energy.
  2. Potential method: Find V (scalar), then energy change ΔU = qΔV, then use ΔK + ΔU = Wapp (or conservation if Wapp = 0).
  3. Big win: adding scalars is simpler than adding vectors, and many geometries give V directly.

Mental model: E is a “slope” (direction + steepness), V is the “height map”.

24.1.1 Potential Energy and Potential

Key ideas

The potential V at a point is defined as potential energy per unit charge for a test charge placed there.

Reference choice: often V = 0 at infinity, but you can choose any convenient zero.

If the electric force does work W on the particle–source system, the potential energy changes by ΔU = −W.

Conservative forces: work depends only on endpoints ⇒ ΔU and ΔV are path independent.

Core relationships

ΔV = Vf − Vi,    ΔU = qΔV,    Welectric = −ΔU
Quantity Meaning Units
V Electric potential (scalar) V (volt) = J/C
U Electric potential energy J
q Charge C
Worked Example: Sign check (what happens to U when a positive charge moves to lower V?)

Given: q > 0 and the particle moves so that V decreases (ΔV < 0).

  1. ΔU = qΔV.
  2. q is positive and ΔV is negative ⇒ ΔU < 0.
  3. So the potential energy decreases; the electric field can do positive work.

Conclusion: For q > 0, moving “downhill” in V lowers U.

24.1.2 Electron-Volt (eV)

Key ideas

The electron-volt is an energy unit convenient for microscopic charges. It is the energy gained by a charge of magnitude e moving through 1 volt of potential difference.

Conversions

1 eV = (1.602 × 10−19 C)(1 V) = 1.602 × 10−19 J
Worked Example: How much energy is 2.0 keV in joules?

Given: 2.0 keV = 2.0 × 103 eV.

  1. Convert to joules: E = (2.0 × 103)(1.602 × 10−19) J.
  2. E = 3.204 × 10−16 J.

Answer: 2.0 keV ≈ 3.2 × 10−16 J.

24.1.3 Energy Method (ΔK, Wapp)

Key ideas

No applied force: electric force converts potential energy ↔ kinetic energy.

ΔK = −qΔV

With applied force: external work can add/remove energy.

ΔK = −qΔV + Wapp
Worked Example: Electron accelerated through a potential difference

Problem: An electron starts from rest and moves through ΔV = +1500 V (from lower to higher potential). Find ΔK.

  1. Use ΔK = −qΔV with q = −e.
  2. ΔK = −(−e)(1500 V) = e(1500 V) = 1500 eV.
  3. In joules: ΔK = (1500)(1.602 × 10−19) ≈ 2.40 × 10−16 J.

Answer: ΔK = 1500 eV ≈ 2.4 × 10−16 J.

Sign discipline: ΔV is “final minus initial”. If you swap endpoints, you must also swap the sign. For electrons (q = −e), many signs flip.

24.2 Equipotential Surfaces and the Electric Field

Key ideas

An equipotential surface is a set of points with the same V. Moving a charge along an equipotential does no work by the electric force. Electric field lines cross equipotentials at right angles.

Along equipotential: ΔV = 0 ⇒ W = 0 E ⟂ equipotential surfaces Field points from high V to low V

Important formulas

Vf − Vi = −∫if E · ds
Uniform field (perpendicular distance Δx):   ΔV = −EΔx

24.2.1 Equipotentials and Work

Key ideas

Because the electric force is conservative, the work done by the electric field depends only on the initial and final equipotentials (not the path). If the endpoints are on the same equipotential, the net work is zero.

Worked Example (Concept): Why must E be perpendicular to equipotentials?
  1. If E had a component tangent to an equipotential surface, a charge moving along that surface would feel a force along its motion.
  2. That force would do work, changing the charge’s potential energy.
  3. But along an equipotential, ΔV = 0 ⇒ ΔU = qΔV = 0 ⇒ work by field must be 0.
  4. Therefore the tangential component must be zero; E is perpendicular.

24.2.2 Computing ΔV from E

Key ideas

Potential difference is the line integral of E along a path, with a minus sign. If one path is messy, choose an easier one (same endpoints ⇒ same ΔV).

Worked Example: Uniform field between plates

Problem: A uniform field of magnitude 2.5 × 104 V/m points in the +x direction. What is ΔV from x = 1.0 cm to x = 5.0 cm?

  1. Δx = 0.050 − 0.010 = 0.040 m.
  2. ΔV = −EΔx = −(2.5 × 104)(0.040) = −1.0 × 103 V.

Answer: V decreases by 1000 V in the +x direction.

24.3 Potential Due to a Charged Particle

Key ideas

With V = 0 at infinity, a point charge q creates a potential that falls like 1/r. The sign of V matches the sign of q.

Important formulas

V(r) = (1 / 4πε0) (q / r) = kq/r

24.3.1 Point Charge

Key ideas

Near a +q: V > 0 and gets larger as you approach the charge.

Near a −q: V < 0 and becomes more negative as you approach.

Worked Example: Potential at a point

Problem: What is V at r = 0.20 m from a charge q = +3.0 nC, with V = 0 at infinity?

  1. Use V = kq/r with k = 8.99 × 109 N·m2/C2.
  2. V = (8.99 × 109)(3.0 × 10−9) / 0.20 ≈ 135 V.

Answer: V ≈ +1.35 × 102 V.

24.3.2 Many Charges (Scalar Superposition)

Key ideas

Electric potential adds by ordinary algebra: compute each contribution kqi/ri and sum. (Unlike E, there is no vector addition.)

Important formula

V = Σ (kqi/ri)
Worked Example: Two charges on a line (find where V = 0)

Setup: +q at x = 0 and −q at x = a. Find x where V(x) = 0 on the x-axis (excluding the charges).

  1. V = kq/|x| + k(−q)/|x − a| = kq(1/|x| − 1/|x − a|).
  2. Set V = 0 ⇒ 1/|x| = 1/|x − a| ⇒ |x| = |x − a|.
  3. On the line, this occurs at the midpoint x = a/2 (between the charges).

Answer: V = 0 at x = a/2 (for equal and opposite charges).

“V = 0” does not mean “E = 0”. For +q and −q, the midpoint has V = 0 but the field is not zero.

24.4 Potential Due to an Electric Dipole

Key ideas

Far from a dipole (r ≫ d), the potential falls faster than a single charge: it scales like 1/r2. The sign depends on angle relative to the dipole moment direction.

Important formula (far field)

V(r, θ) = (1 / 4πε0) (p cosθ / r2),   for r ≫ d
Worked Example (Concept): Where is the dipole potential zero?
  1. From V ∝ cosθ, we have V = 0 when cosθ = 0.
  2. So θ = 90°: points on the perpendicular bisector (equatorial line) have zero dipole potential (far away).

Answer: V = 0 on the equatorial line (θ = 90°) in the far-field approximation.

24.5 Potential Due to a Continuous Charge Distribution

Key ideas

For a continuous distribution, split the charge into elements dq, treat each as a point charge contribution, and integrate. Because V is scalar, there are no components to resolve.

dV = k dq / r V = ∫ dV Use λ, σ for dq

24.5.1 General Integral

Important formula

V = (1 / 4πε0) ∫ (dq / r)

Choosing dq

Distribution Density dq
Line (1D) λ (C/m) dq = λ dx
Surface (2D) σ (C/m2) dq = σ dA
Volume (3D) ρ (C/m3) dq = ρ dV

24.5.2 Line of Charge (Setup)

Key ideas

The hard part is the geometry: write r(x) from the element dq to the field point, then integrate k dq/r. Symmetry can reduce a 2D integral to 1D.

Worked Example: Uniform line segment on its perpendicular bisector (setup only)

Setup: Rod from x = −L/2 to +L/2 with linear density λ. Find V at point a distance d on the perpendicular bisector.

  1. Choose dq = λ dx.
  2. Distance to the point: r = √(x2 + d2).
  3. Integrand: dV = k (λ dx)/√(x2 + d2).
  4. Use symmetry: integrate from 0 to L/2 and double.

Checkpoint: If λ > 0 and V = 0 at infinity, V must be positive.

24.6 Calculating the Field from the Potential

Key ideas

The electric field is the negative gradient of V: it points in the direction where V decreases most rapidly. In 1D, this becomes “minus the slope” of V(x).

Important formulas

Es = −∂V/∂s
Ex = −∂V/∂x,   Ey = −∂V/∂y,   Ez = −∂V/∂z
Uniform field:   E = −ΔV/Δs

24.6.1 Components from Derivatives

Worked Example: Given V(x), find Ex(x)

Given: V(x) = 50x2 − 20x (V), with x in meters. Find Ex(x).

  1. Ex = −dV/dx.
  2. dV/dx = 100x − 20.
  3. Ex(x) = −(100x − 20) = 20 − 100x (V/m).

Answer: Ex(x) = 20 − 100x V/m.

Common mistake: forgetting the minus sign. If V increases with x, the field points in −x.

24.7 Electric Potential Energy of a System of Charged Particles

Key ideas

System potential energy U equals the work required to assemble the charges from infinity (with charges initially at rest). For two charges, U depends on their product and separation.

Important formula

U = (1 / 4πε0) (q1q2/r) = k q1q2/r

24.7.1 Two Charges

Worked Example: Sign and magnitude of U

Problem: Two charges q1 = +2.0 nC and q2 = −3.0 nC are separated by r = 0.50 m. Find U.

  1. Use U = k q1q2/r.
  2. q1q2 = (2.0×10−9)(−3.0×10−9) = −6.0×10−18 C2.
  3. U = (8.99×109)(−6.0×10−18)/0.50 ≈ −1.08×10−7 J.

Answer: U ≈ −1.1 × 10−7 J (negative because opposite charges attract).

24.7.2 Many Charges

Key ideas

For multiple charges, the total U can be written as a sum over pairs (count each pair once), or as U = (1/2) Σ qi V( ri ) where V excludes the “self” potential of charge i.

Worked Example (Strategy): Fast way to compute U for three charges

Idea: Use pairwise energy.

  1. Compute U12 = kq1q2/r12, U13 = kq1q3/r13, U23 = kq2q3/r23.
  2. Total: U = U12 + U13 + U23.

Checkpoint: Units must be joules; signs come from charge products.

Study tip: If you can find V, use energy first: compute ΔV, then ΔU = qΔV, then relate to ΔK. Remember: E points “downhill” in V and is perpendicular to equipotential surfaces.
