32.1 Gauss’ Law for Magnetic Fields

Key ideas

Magnetic field lines form closed loops. Unlike electric charge, there is no known isolated “magnetic charge” (no magnetic monopoles). This is encoded in Gauss’ law for magnetism.

Dipoles are fundamental Net magnetic flux through closed surface = 0 Field lines never start/stop

Gauss’ law (magnetism)

∮ B · dA = 0

Same idea as “what enters must leave” for B through a closed surface.

32.1.1 No Magnetic Monopoles

Practical consequence

Break a bar magnet into two pieces and each piece becomes a smaller magnet with its own north and south poles. You cannot isolate a “north-only” magnet.

32.1.2 Maxwell’s Equations (Snapshot)

Integral form (most used in this course)

Law Equation Meaning
Gauss (electric) ∮ E · dA = Qenc/ε0 Charges are sources/sinks of E
Gauss (magnetic) ∮ B · dA = 0 No monopoles; closed B loops
Faraday ∮ E · ds = − dΦB/dt Changing ΦB induces circulating E
Ampère–Maxwell ∮ B · ds = μ0ienc + μ0ε0dΦE/dt Currents and changing ΦE produce B

32.2 Induced Magnetic Fields

Key ideas

Faraday’s law says a changing magnetic flux induces an electric field. Maxwell found the “partner” idea: a changing electric flux induces a magnetic field, completing the symmetry between electricity and magnetism.

32.2.1 Ampère–Maxwell Law

Combined law

∮ B · ds = μ0ienc + μ0ε0dΦE/dt

The added term μ0ε0dΦE/dt fixes an inconsistency you would get using Ampère’s law alone for a charging capacitor: different surfaces spanning the same loop would predict different fields unless the changing electric flux is included.

32.2.2 Field Around a Charging Capacitor

Circular symmetry result (between plates)

B(2πr) = μ0id,enc

For a parallel-plate capacitor (plate radius R) being charged so that the displacement current is uniformly “spread” over the plate area, the enclosed displacement current scales like area: id,enc = i(r2/R2) for r ≤ R.

Worked Example: B-field inside a charging capacitor

Given: A circular loop of radius r = 2.0 cm lies between plates of radius R = 6.0 cm. The conduction current charging the capacitor is i = 0.60 A (steady at that instant).

  1. For r ≤ R: id,enc = i(r2/R2) = 0.60(0.022/0.062) = 0.60(1/9) = 0.0667 A.
  2. B = μ0id,enc/(2πr) = (4π×10−7)(0.0667)/(2π·0.020).
  3. B = 6.7×10−7 T.

Answer: B ≈ 6.7×10−7 T at r = 2.0 cm.

32.3 Displacement Current

Key idea

In regions with no conduction current (like the gap between capacitor plates), a time-changing electric field still produces magnetic effects. Maxwell’s “displacement current” term is what makes the magnetic field prediction consistent everywhere.

32.3.1 Definition and Consistency Check

Displacement current

id = ε0 dΦE/dt

Parallel-plate capacitor (useful shortcut)

i = C dV/dt = ε0(A/d) dV/dt
Worked Example: Displacement current equals conduction current

Given: A parallel-plate capacitor has A = 5.0×10−3 m2, plate separation d = 1.0 mm. The voltage increases at dV/dt = 20 V/s.

  1. C = ε0A/d = (8.85×10−12)(5.0×10−3)/(1.0×10−3) = 4.43×10−11 F.
  2. i = C dV/dt = (4.43×10−11)(20) = 8.9×10−10 A.

Answer: i = id ≈ 8.9×10−10 A.

32.4 Magnets

Key ideas

The simplest magnetic structure is a dipole (north–south pair). A small current loop is also a magnetic dipole. In external fields, dipoles experience torque and have orientation-dependent potential energy.

τ = μ × B
U = −μ · B = −μB cosθ

32.4.1 Dipole Moment, Torque, and Energy

Current loop magnetic moment

μ = NiA
Worked Example: Torque and energy of a dipole

Given: A loop has μ = 0.040 A·m2 in a uniform B = 0.50 T field. The angle is θ = 60°.

  1. Torque magnitude: τ = μB sinθ = (0.040)(0.50)sin60° = 0.0173 N·m.
  2. Potential energy: U = −μB cosθ = −(0.040)(0.50)(0.50) = −0.010 J.

Answer: τ ≈ 1.7×10−2 N·m, U = −1.0×10−2 J.

32.5 Magnetism and Electrons

Key ideas

Atomic-scale magnetism largely comes from electron orbital motion (a current-loop-like effect) and electron spin (intrinsic angular momentum). Many material behaviors follow from how these moments respond to an external field.

32.5.1 Spin and Orbital Magnetic Moments

Common proportionalities

μorb = −(e/2m)L
μs ≈ −(e/m)S    (spin g ≈ 2)

Magnitudes are often expressed in units of the Bohr magneton: μB = eħ/(2me) ≈ 9.27×10−24 J/T.

32.5.2 Magnetization and Susceptibility

Definitions (linear materials)

M = χmH
B = μ0(H + M) = μ0(1 + χm)H

χm < 0 → diamagnetic (weakly repelled). χm > 0 → paramagnetic (weakly attracted) or ferromagnetic (strongly attracted, nonlinear).

32.6 Diamagnetism

Key ideas

Diamagnetism is a weak, universal response of bound electrons: an applied magnetic field slightly changes electron motion, inducing a magnetic moment that opposes the applied field (Lenz-like behavior on the atomic scale).

χm < 0 Weak repulsion in nonuniform fields Temperature-independent (often)

32.6.1 χm < 0 and Weak Repulsion

Field relations

μ = μ0(1 + χm)    (with χm negative)

In a nonuniform field, a diamagnetic object tends to move toward regions of weaker field (it is “pushed out” of strong-field regions).

32.7 Paramagnetism

Key ideas

Paramagnetic materials have atoms with permanent magnetic moments (often from unpaired electron spins). In an external field, these moments partially align with the field, giving a small positive susceptibility.

χm > 0 (small) Alignment competes with thermal agitation

32.7.1 Alignment vs Temperature (Curie Law)

Typical dependence (weak fields)

χm = C/T

Higher temperature randomizes orientations more strongly, reducing net alignment and thus reducing χm.

32.8 Ferromagnetism

Key ideas

Ferromagnets (iron, cobalt, nickel, etc.) can have very large magnetization because quantum interactions favor parallel alignment of neighboring magnetic moments, producing domains of aligned spins.

Large, nonlinear response Domains + domain walls Hysteresis and remanence

32.8.1 Domains and Hysteresis

B–H behavior (qualitative)

When you increase H, domains grow/reorient and B rises rapidly, then approaches saturation. If you reduce H back to zero, B does not generally return to zero (remanent magnetization). You must apply a reverse field to bring B back to zero (coercive field).

Study tip: for Maxwell-law problems, decide which flux changes (ΦE or ΦB), pick a symmetry-friendly loop/surface, and write the integral law. For magnetism-of-matter problems, separate microscopic moment (electron physics) from macroscopic response (M, χm, domains).
