15.1 Simple Harmonic Motion

SHM occurs when the restoring force is proportional to displacement and opposite in direction: F = −kx. The result is sinusoidal motion with a single natural frequency.

F = −kx x is sinusoidal ω is constant

Core forms

x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)
ω = √(k/m)
T = 2π/ω

15.2 Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

In ideal SHM (no damping), total mechanical energy stays constant and swaps between kinetic and potential forms.

Mass–spring energy

E = (1/2)kA2 = (1/2)kx2 + (1/2)mv2

Maximum speed occurs at equilibrium: vmax = ωA.

15.3 An Angular Simple Harmonic Oscillator

Rotational analog of SHM: a restoring torque proportional to angular displacement gives sinusoidal angular motion.

Torsion oscillator

τ = −κθ
ω = √(κ/I)

κ is torsion constant; I is rotational inertia about the axis.

15.4 Pendulums, Circular Motion

For small angular displacements, the restoring torque on a simple pendulum is approximately linear in angle, leading to SHM.

Small-angle simple pendulum

ω ≈ √(g/L)
T ≈ 2π√(L/g)

The approximation requires small angles (typically < 10–15° for good accuracy).

15.5 Damped Simple Harmonic Motion

Real oscillators lose energy to friction/drag. Damping makes the amplitude decay in time; depending on damping strength, the system may still oscillate (underdamped) or return without oscillation (overdamped).

Underdamped qualitative form

x(t) = A e−bt/2m cos(ωdt + φ)

ωd is the damped angular frequency; b is the damping constant (in a linear drag model).

15.6 Forced Oscillations and Resonance

A driven oscillator responds most strongly when the driving frequency is near the natural frequency. Damping limits the peak amplitude and sets the resonance width (quality factor idea).

Resonance idea

Driving frequency near ω0 ⇒ largest steady-state amplitude
Worked example: identify resonance condition

If a mass–spring system has k = 200 N/m and m = 0.50 kg, then ω0 = √(k/m) = √(400) = 20 rad/s, so the resonance is near f0 = ω0/(2π) = 3.18 Hz.

Answer: Drive near 3.2 Hz for the largest response (with damping limiting the peak).

Study tip: for oscillations, keep the “translation table” in mind: restoring force/torque sets ω, energy sets amplitude, and damping/driving sets how amplitude evolves in time.
