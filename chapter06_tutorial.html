6.1 Friction

Friction is a contact force that resists relative motion (or impending motion) between surfaces. Static friction adjusts as needed up to a maximum; kinetic friction is roughly constant during sliding.

Models

fs ≤ μsN
fk = μkN

Direction: opposite the relative motion (kinetic) or impending motion (static).

6.2 The Drag Force and Terminal Speed

Drag is a resistive force from motion through a fluid. At low speeds it can be proportional to v; at higher speeds it often scales like v². Terminal speed occurs when drag balances weight (net force zero).

Common forms

Fd = bv
Fd = cv2
Terminal speed: mg = Fd

6.3 Uniform Circular Motion

Uniform circular motion has constant speed but changing velocity direction, so acceleration is nonzero and points toward the center (centripetal).

Centripetal acceleration and force

ac = v2/r = ω2r
Fnet,radial = mv2/r
Workflow tip: radial axis

Draw a free-body diagram, choose “radial inward” as +, and apply ΣFradial = mv²/r. Don’t invent a “centripetal force”; it’s the net inward force from real forces.

Study tip: for circular motion, always start with mv²/r and identify which real forces point inward/outward. For drag, find the balance point for terminal speed by setting net force to zero.
