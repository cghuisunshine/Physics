42.1 Discovering the Nucleus

Key ideas

Rutherford’s gold-foil scattering showed atoms are mostly empty space: most alpha particles pass through, but a tiny fraction scatter through large angles. That implies a small, dense, positively charged nucleus.

Atoms mostly empty Small positive nucleus Coulomb repulsion

Scale

Atomic size is ~10−10 m, nuclear size is ~10−15 m. The nucleus is ~105 times smaller in radius, so ~1015 times smaller in volume.

42.1.1 Closest Approach in Rutherford Scattering

Head-on (simplified) estimate

K = (1/4πϵ01Z2e2)/rmin ⇒ rmin = (1/4πϵ01Z2e2)/K

For an alpha particle, Z1 = 2. For a target nucleus, Z2 = Z.

Worked Example: 5.0 MeV alpha on gold (Z = 79)

Given: K = 5.0 MeV, Z1Z2 = 2·79 = 158. Use (1/4πϵ02 = 1.44 MeV·fm.

  1. rmin = (1.44 MeV·fm)(158)/(5.0 MeV) = 45.5 fm.
  2. Convert: 45.5 fm = 4.55×10−14 m.

Answer: rmin ≈ 4.6×10−14 m (still larger than a typical nuclear radius, so “hard” hits are rare).

42.2 Some Nuclear Properties

Key ideas

Nuclei are built from protons (Z) and neutrons (N). The mass number is A = Z + N. Isotopes share Z but differ in N. Nuclear size and binding energy show that the strong nuclear force is short-range and saturating.

42.2.1 Radius and Density

Empirical radius law

R = R0A1/3, with R0 ≈ 1.2 fm

Because volume scales as R3 ∝ A, nuclear density is roughly constant across the periodic table.

Quick estimate: gold nucleus radius (A ≈ 197)

R ≈ 1.2 fm · 1971/3 ≈ 7.0 fm.

42.2.2 Worked Example: He-4 Binding Energy

Mass defect → binding energy

Eb = Δmc2, 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c2
Δm = ZmH + Nmn − matom

Using atomic masses lets electron masses cancel cleanly (mH includes one electron).

Compute Eb and Eb/A for helium-4

Given: mH = 1.007825 u, mn = 1.008665 u, m(He-4 atom) = 4.002603 u. Z = 2, N = 2.

  1. Δm = 2mH + 2mn − m(He) = 0.030377 u.
  2. Eb = (0.030377 u)(931.5 MeV/u) = 28.3 MeV.
  3. Eb/A = 28.3/4 = 7.07 MeV per nucleon.

Answer: He-4 binding energy is ≈ 28.3 MeV (≈ 7.1 MeV/nucleon), explaining its stability.

42.3 Radioactive Decay

Key ideas

Radioactive decay is a spontaneous transformation of an unstable nucleus. The decay of a large sample is predictable statistically: each nucleus has a constant probability per unit time to decay.

42.3.1 Decay Law, Activity, Half-life

Core equations

N(t) = N0e−λt
A(t) = −dN/dt = λN(t)
t1/2 = (ln 2)/λ

Activity A is measured in becquerel (Bq), where 1 Bq = 1 decay/s. A convenient “mental model” is that every half-life multiplies N and A by 1/2.

42.3.2 Worked Example: Activity After 3 Half-lives

Compute activity after 30 days for t1/2 = 10 days

Given: N0 = 1.0×1020 nuclei, half-life t1/2 = 10 days, and t = 30 days.

  1. After 3 half-lives, N = N0/23 = N0/8 = 1.25×1019.
  2. λ = ln 2 / t1/2 = 0.693/(10 days) = 0.0693 day−1 = 8.02×10−7 s−1.
  3. A(t) = λN = (8.02×10−7 s−1)(1.25×1019) = 1.00×1013 Bq.

Answer: After 30 days, A ≈ 1×1013 Bq (and it is 1/8 of the initial activity).

42.4 Alpha Decay

Key ideas

In alpha decay, a nucleus emits an alpha particle (a tightly bound He-4 nucleus). Classically, the alpha particle would be trapped by the Coulomb barrier, but quantum tunneling allows escape. The decay energy Q comes from the mass difference between initial and final states.

42.4.1 Q-value and Recoil Sharing

Energy split (two-body decay)

Q = (mparent − mdaughter − mα)c2
Kα ≈ Q · mdaughter/(mdaughter + mα)
Worked Example: How much of Q goes to the alpha?

Given: Q = 5.4 MeV, daughter mass ≈ 206 u, alpha mass ≈ 4 u.

  1. Kα ≈ Q · 206/(206 + 4) = 5.4·206/210 = 5.30 MeV.
  2. The recoil kinetic energy of the daughter is Q − Kα ≈ 0.10 MeV.

Answer: For heavy nuclei, the alpha carries almost all of Q; the daughter recoil is small.

42.5 Beta Decay

Key ideas

In beta decay, a neutron and proton can transform into each other via the weak interaction. A key signature is a continuous electron/positron energy spectrum, explained by the emission of a neutrino (or antineutrino) that carries away some energy and momentum.

42.5.1 β−, β+, and Electron Capture

Three related processes

Process What changes Example form
β− decay n → p (Z increases by 1) (A, Z) → (A, Z + 1) + e− + ν̄
β+ decay p → n (Z decreases by 1) (A, Z) → (A, Z − 1) + e+ + ν
Electron capture p + e− → n (Z decreases by 1) (A, Z) + e− → (A, Z − 1) + ν

In β+ decay, the available energy Q must also cover creation of the positron (and, effectively, an electron when comparing atomic masses), so β+ often competes with electron capture.

42.6 Radioactive Dating

Key ideas

If you know the half-life of an isotope and can estimate how much was present initially (or compare parent/daughter amounts), you can infer the time elapsed. Dating is an application of the same exponential decay law.

42.6.1 Worked Example: Carbon-14 Age

Find the age if 25% of the original C-14 remains

Given: Carbon-14 half-life t1/2 = 5730 years. A sample has N/N0 = 0.25.

  1. 0.25 = (1/2)2, so two half-lives have passed.
  2. t = 2t1/2 = 2(5730 years) = 11,460 years.

Answer: The sample is about 1.15×104 years old.

42.7 Measuring Radiation Dosage

Key ideas

Dosage quantifies energy deposited in tissue and the biological damage potential. Different radiation types cause different damage per joule absorbed, so we distinguish absorbed dose and equivalent dose.

42.7.1 Gray, Sievert, Weighting Factor

Definitions

absorbed dose: D = (energy absorbed)/(mass) (1 Gy = 1 J/kg)
equivalent dose: H = wRD (unit: Sv)

wR is a radiation weighting factor (roughly: alpha ~20, beta/gamma ~1).

Worked Example: Convert absorbed dose to equivalent dose

Given: D = 0.050 Gy from alpha particles (wR = 20).

  1. H = wRD = 20(0.050 Gy) = 1.0 Sv.

Answer: 0.050 Gy of alpha radiation corresponds to 1.0 Sv equivalent dose.

42.8 Nuclear Models

Key ideas

No single simple picture captures all nuclear behavior. Two complementary models are widely used: the liquid-drop model (collective behavior, fission) and the shell model (single-particle levels, magic numbers).

42.8.1 Liquid-drop vs Shell Model

Two viewpoints

Model Picture Explains well
Liquid-drop Nucleus as a drop of nuclear fluid with surface tension Binding-energy trends, fission, collective modes
Shell model Nucleons occupy quantized energy levels in a potential Magic numbers, spins/parities, extra stability

The famous binding-energy-per-nucleon curve peaks near iron: fusion releases energy for light nuclei; fission releases energy for very heavy nuclei.

Study tip: always start nuclear problems with bookkeeping: identify (A, Z), write the reaction/decay with conservation of A and charge, then use mass differences for Q and exponential decay for time.
