31.1 LC Oscillations

Key ideas

In an ideal LC circuit (no resistance), energy sloshes back and forth between the capacitor’s electric field and the inductor’s magnetic field. Charge on the capacitor and current in the inductor are sinusoidal and 90° out of phase.

Energy conserved q and i oscillate ω depends on L and C

Circuit–oscillator analogy: q ↔ x (displacement), i = dq/dt ↔ v, 1/C ↔ k (spring constant), L ↔ m.

31.1.1 Frequency and Time Dependence

Natural angular frequency

ω0 = 1/√(LC)

Charge and current

q(t) = Q cos(ω0t + ϕ)
i(t) = dq/dt = −ω0Q sin(ω0t + ϕ)
Worked Example: Period of LC oscillations

Given: L = 2.0 mH, C = 5.0 μF.

  1. ω0 = 1/√(LC) = 1/√(2.0×10−3 · 5.0×10−6) = 1/√(1.0×10−8) = 1.0×104 rad/s.
  2. T = 2π/ω0 = 2π/104 = 6.28×10−4 s.

Answer: T ≈ 0.628 ms.

31.1.2 Energy Exchange

Instantaneous energies

UE = q2/(2C)     UB = ½Li2

Total energy (constant for ideal LC)

U = UE + UB = Q2/(2C) = ½LI2

When q is maximum (capacitor “fully charged”), i = 0 and all energy is electric. When q = 0, i is maximum and all energy is magnetic.

31.2 Damped Oscillations in an RLC Circuit

Key ideas

Adding resistance converts some electrical energy into thermal energy. Oscillations can persist (underdamped), just barely avoid oscillating (critical), or return without oscillating (overdamped).

31.2.1 Damping Regimes

RLC charge equation

L d2q/dt2 + R dq/dt + q/C = 0

Helpful parameters

ω0 = 1/√(LC)     α = R/(2L)
Case Condition Behavior
Underdamped α < ω0 Oscillates with decaying amplitude
Critical α = ω0 Fastest non-oscillatory return
Overdamped α > ω0 Non-oscillatory, slower return

31.2.2 Underdamped Solution

Charge vs time

q(t) = Q e−αt cos(ω′t + ϕ)
ω′ = √(ω02 − α2)
Worked Example: Damped frequency

Given: L = 0.50 H, C = 8.0 μF, R = 20 Ω (assume underdamped).

  1. ω0 = 1/√(LC) = 1/√(0.50·8.0×10−6) = 1/√(4.0×10−6) = 500 rad/s.
  2. α = R/(2L) = 20/(1.0) = 20 s−1.
  3. ω′ = √(5002 − 202) ≈ √(250000 − 400) ≈ 499.6 rad/s.

Answer: ω′ ≈ 5.00×102 rad/s (slightly below ω0).

31.3 Forced Oscillations of Three Simple Circuits

Key ideas

With a sinusoidal driving emf, the circuit settles into a steady-state oscillation at the driving frequency. The amplitude depends strongly on frequency and peaks near resonance.

31.3.1 Driven Response and Resonance

Driving emf (typical form)

ℰ(t) = ℰm sin(ωt)

Damping (resistance) broadens the resonance and limits the maximum response. In the mechanical analogy, resistance plays the role of friction.

31.3.2 Bandwidth and Quality

Series RLC quality factor (common definition)

Q = ω0L/R

Bandwidth (narrow resonance approximation)

Δω ≈ R/L

Larger Q means sharper resonance (smaller Δω) and slower energy loss per cycle.

31.4 The Series RLC Circuit

Key ideas

In AC steady state, the resistor, capacitor, and inductor act like frequency-dependent “impedances”. The current amplitude is ℰm/Z, where Z combines R and the net reactance.

XL = ωL     XC = 1/(ωC)

Inductor “reactance” increases with ω; capacitor reactance decreases with ω.

31.4.1 Impedance and Phase

Impedance magnitude

Z = √(R2 + (XL − XC)2) = √(R2 + (ωL − 1/(ωC))2)

Phase angle (emf leads current by φ)

tanφ = (XL − XC)/R
Worked Example: Current amplitude and phase

Given: R = 30 Ω, L = 0.20 H, C = 50 μF, f = 60 Hz, ℰm = 120 V.

  1. ω = 2πf = 377 rad/s.
  2. XL = ωL = (377)(0.20) = 75.4 Ω; XC = 1/(ωC) = 1/(377·50×10−6) = 53.1 Ω.
  3. Z = √(302 + (75.4 − 53.1)2) = √(900 + 22.32) ≈ 37.4 Ω.
  4. Im = ℰm/Z = 120/37.4 = 3.21 A.
  5. φ = tan−1((22.3)/30) ≈ 36.7° (net inductive, current lags).

Answer: Im ≈ 3.21 A, φ ≈ 36.7° (lagging).

31.4.2 Resonance

Resonant condition

XL = XC   ⇒   ω0 = 1/√(LC)

At resonance

Z = R     φ = 0     Im = ℰm/R

Even at resonance, the inductor and capacitor can each have large voltage amplitudes (they can “share” large reactive voltages) even though the net reactance is zero.

31.5 Power in Alternating-Current Circuits

Key ideas

Only the resistor dissipates energy on average. Inductors and capacitors exchange energy with the source but return it over a cycle, giving zero average power for ideal reactive elements.

31.5.1 RMS Values

For sinusoidal signals

Vrms = Vm/√2     Irms = Im/√2

RMS is the “equivalent DC” value that would deliver the same average power to a resistor.

31.5.2 Average Power and Power Factor

Average power

Pavg = VrmsIrmscosφ = Irms2R

Power factor

power factor = cosφ
Worked Example: Average power in a series RLC load

Given: Vrms = 120 V, Irms = 2.5 A, φ = 30°.

  1. Pavg = VrmsIrmscosφ = 120·2.5·cos30°.
  2. Pavg = 300·0.866 = 260 W.

Answer: Pavg ≈ 260 W, power factor = 0.866.

31.6 Transformers

Key ideas

A transformer uses mutual induction to change AC voltages. In an ideal transformer (no losses), power is conserved: increasing voltage decreases current by the same factor.

31.6.1 Ideal Transformer Ratios

Voltage ratio

Vs/Vp = Ns/Np

Current ratio (ideal)

Is/Ip = Np/Ns
Worked Example: Step-up transformer

Given: Vp = 120 V (rms), Np = 500 turns, Ns = 2500 turns, load draws Is = 0.80 A (rms).

  1. Vs = Vp(Ns/Np) = 120(2500/500) = 600 V.
  2. Ip = Is(Ns/Np) = 0.80(2500/500) = 4.0 A.

Answer: Vs = 600 V, Ip = 4.0 A (ideal).

31.6.2 Power Transmission Idea

Why step up voltage?

For a given power P, a higher transmission voltage means lower current (P = VI). Since resistive line losses scale as Ploss = I2R, reducing I greatly reduces wasted heating.

High V → low I Loss ∝ I2 Transformers enable efficient grids

Study tip: in AC problems, separate geometry (R, L, C) from frequency (ω). Compute XL, XC, then Z and φ, then convert to rms and average power if needed.
