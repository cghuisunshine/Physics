11.1 Rolling as Translation and Rotation Combined

For rolling without slipping, translation of the center of mass and rotation about the center are linked. The no-slip constraint is the key equation that connects linear and angular motion.

No slip v = ωR a = αR

No-slip condition

vcm = ωR

11.2 Forces and Kinetic Energy of Rolling

Static friction can be essential for rolling without slipping, even though it does no work at the point of contact (for pure rolling on a rigid surface).

Total kinetic energy (rolling rigid body)

K = (1/2)Mvcm2 + (1/2)Icmω2

Using v = ωR often lets you write everything in terms of v or ω.

11.3 The Yo-Yo

A yo-yo is a great example of translation + rotation with a string tension producing both a net force and a torque about the center.

Workflow: ΣF gives acm, Στ about center gives α, and the string constraint relates a and α.

11.4 Torque Revisited

Torque measures the “rotational effectiveness” of a force about a chosen origin or axis. Choosing the origin wisely can simplify a problem dramatically.

Torque magnitude

τ = rF sin θ

Vector form: τ = r × F.

11.5 Angular Momentum

Angular momentum depends on the choice of origin. For a particle, it’s the cross product of position and linear momentum.

Particle definition

L = r × p

11.6 Newton’s Second Law in Angular Form

Torque is the time rate of change of angular momentum. This is the rotational analog of F = dp/dt.

τnet = dL/dt

11.7 Angular Momentum of a Rigid Body

For rotation about a fixed axis, angular momentum aligns with the axis and has magnitude L = Iω. In more general 3D motion, L and ω may not be parallel.

L = Iω (fixed-axis rotation)

11.8 Conservation of Angular Momentum

If external torque about a chosen axis is zero, angular momentum about that axis is conserved. This explains spin-up when a skater pulls in arms (I decreases, ω increases).

τext = 0 ⇒ L = constant
Worked example: skater spin-up

If I halves, conservation L = Iω implies ω doubles. Rotational kinetic energy usually increases; the extra energy comes from internal work.

11.9 Precession of a Gyroscope

A torque applied to a spinning object changes the direction of its angular momentum, causing precession: the spin axis slowly rotates around the vertical.

Precession rate (steady, small tilt)

Ω = τ/L

For gravity torque on a top: τ ≈ rMg and L ≈ Iω.

Study tip: for rolling problems, start with the constraint (v = ωR) and energy. For torque/L problems, decide the axis first and check whether external torque is zero (conservation).
