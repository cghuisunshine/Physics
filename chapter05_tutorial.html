5.1 Newton’s First and Second Laws

Newton’s first law defines inertial frames: if net force is zero, velocity is constant. Newton’s second law quantifies dynamics: acceleration is proportional to net force.

∑F = ma

∑F uses only real forces on the object, not “ma as a force.”

5.2 Some Particular Forces

Most introductory mechanics problems use a small set of forces. The key is to recognize them and draw them correctly.

Force Direction Typical magnitude
Weight Downward mg
Normal Perpendicular to surface Depends on constraints
Tension Along rope/string Same along ideal massless rope
Friction Along surface, opposes motion μN (model)

5.3 Applying Newton’s Laws

The standard method: draw a free-body diagram (FBD), choose axes, write ΣF in each axis direction, and solve simultaneously.

Checklist: free-body diagram
  1. Isolate the object and draw only forces acting on it.
  2. Pick convenient axes (often along incline or along motion).
  3. Resolve forces into components; then write ΣF = ma for each axis.
  4. Use constraints (rope length, contact, etc.) to relate accelerations.

Study tip: if a problem feels messy, the FBD is usually wrong or axes are poorly chosen. Fix the diagram first, then the algebra becomes routine.
