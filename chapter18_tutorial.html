18.1 Temperature

Key ideas

Temperature is a property we assign to systems that are in thermal equilibrium with each other. When two objects are put in contact, energy may transfer until equilibrium is reached; then both share a common temperature.

Thermal equilibrium Temperature is a state variable Measured by a “thermometric” property

Thermometric property

A thermometer uses some measurable property that changes predictably with temperature (length of a liquid column, resistance of a wire, brightness of radiation, etc.) and is calibrated using fixed points.

Don’t confuse: temperature (how “hot”) vs heat Q (energy transfer due to ΔT).

18.1.1 Thermal Equilibrium and Thermometers

Zeroth law (operational meaning of temperature)

If A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is in thermal equilibrium with C.

This lets us use B as a thermometer: if B equilibrates with A and with C, then A and C share the same temperature.

Problem-solving pattern: “equilibrium implies equality”
  1. Identify what is in thermal contact and what is insulated.
  2. Equilibrium condition: equal temperature at the interface (for conduction problems) or a common final temperature (for mixing).
  3. Then apply energy conservation to solve for that temperature.

18.1.2 Kelvin Scale and Absolute Zero

Conversions

T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15
ΔT in K = ΔT in °C

Absolute zero is 0 K = −273.15 °C. Use Kelvin for thermodynamics (especially when formulas involve ratios like T2/T1).

18.2 The Celsius and Fahrenheit Scales

Fixed points and linear scales

Many temperature scales are defined by choosing fixed points (like the freezing and boiling points of water at 1 atm) and assigning numbers to them. A linear scale assumes equal temperature intervals correspond to equal numeric intervals.

Event (1 atm) Celsius Fahrenheit Kelvin
Freezing of water 0 °C 32 °F 273.15 K
Boiling of water 100 °C 212 °F 373.15 K

18.2.1 Linear Conversion Between Scales

Celsius ↔ Fahrenheit

T(°F) = (9/5)T(°C) + 32
T(°C) = (5/9)(T(°F) − 32)

For any linear scales X and Y, a convenient method is to map both onto a common fractional position between two fixed points: (T − T1)/(T2 − T1).

18.2.2 Worked Example: Convert Fahrenheit to Celsius

Human body temperature: 98.6 °F in °C

Given: T = 98.6 °F.

  1. Use T(°C) = (5/9)(T(°F) − 32).
  2. T(°C) = (5/9)(98.6 − 32) = (5/9)(66.6) = 37.0 °C.

Answer: 98.6 °F corresponds to 37.0 °C.

18.3 Thermal Expansion

Key idea

Most materials expand when heated because the average separation between atoms/molecules increases. For modest temperature changes, expansion is approximately proportional to the initial size and to ΔT.

Small ΔT → linear approximation Coefficients depend on material Constraints can create thermal stress

18.3.1 Linear Expansion (Solids)

Model

ΔL = αL0ΔT

α is the coefficient of linear expansion (units 1/K or 1/°C). This approximation works when αΔT is small.

Material (typical) α (10−6/K) Notes
Steel 11 Structural metals often in 10–20 range
Aluminum 23 Expands about twice as much as steel
Glass 9 Lower expansion; can still crack under thermal gradients

If an object is prevented from expanding, it can develop large forces/stresses. Real engineering designs include expansion joints.

18.3.2 Volume Expansion and Density Change

Volume expansion

ΔV = βV0ΔT

For many solids, β ≈ 3α. For liquids, use β directly (linear expansion is not meaningful for a fluid).

Density change (mass fixed)

ρ = m/V, so Δρ/ρ ≈ −ΔV/V = −βΔT

Warmer material at the same mass tends to have lower density.

18.3.3 Worked Example: Expansion of a Rod

How much does a 2.0 m rod expand when heated by 180 K?

Given: L0 = 2.0 m, α = 2.3×10−5/K, ΔT = 180 K.

  1. Use ΔL = αL0ΔT.
  2. ΔL = (2.3×10−5)(2.0)(180) = 8.28×10−3 m.

Answer: ΔL ≈ 8.3 mm.

18.4 Absorption of Heat

Big picture

Heat Q is energy transferred across a boundary because of a temperature difference. It is not “contained” in an object the way internal energy is.

Heat flows due to ΔT Q is path-dependent Internal energy is a state variable

Core relations

Q = CΔT
Q = mcΔT
Q = mL

C: heat capacity, c: specific heat, L: latent heat (fusion/vaporization, etc.).

18.4.1 Heat Is Energy Transfer (Units + Sign)

Sign convention

Q > 0: energy enters the system as heat   |   Q < 0: energy leaves

“System” means whatever object or collection you choose to analyze; be consistent.

Units

Unit Meaning Conversion
joule (J) SI unit of energy —
calorie (cal) Historically: heat to raise 1 g water by 1 °C 1 cal = 4.186 J
food Calorie (Cal) kilocalorie 1 Cal = 1 kcal = 4186 J

18.4.2 Heat Capacity and Specific Heat

Definitions

C = Q/ΔT
c = Q/(mΔT) so Q = mcΔT

C depends on the object (how much stuff, shape, etc.). Specific heat c is a material property per unit mass.

Use ΔT = Tf − Ti with a sign. If an object cools, ΔT < 0 and the computed Q will be negative.

18.4.3 Calorimetry and Phase Change

Mixing problems (energy conservation)

ΣQ = 0   (heat lost by hot parts + heat gained by cold parts + container = 0)

Often you assume the container is insulated from the outside during mixing (no net heat with the room) and that the final state has a common temperature Tf.

Phase change

Q = mL

During melting/freezing/boiling/condensation, temperature stays constant while energy goes into (or comes from) breaking/forming intermolecular structure.

Checklist: “stage” the energy accounting
  1. List possible stages: warm/cool within a phase, then melt/freeze, then warm/cool liquid, etc.
  2. At each stage, compute the energy needed/available.
  3. Stop when the energy budget runs out; that tells you the final phase and temperature.

18.4.4 Worked Example: Ice in Warm Water

Will all the ice melt, and what is the final temperature?

Given: 0.200 kg liquid water at 20.0 °C mixed with 0.050 kg ice at 0 °C in an insulated cup. Use cw = 4186 J/(kg·K), Lf = 3.33×105 J/kg.

  1. Heat the warm water can release by cooling to 0 °C: Qcool = mcΔT = (0.200)(4186)(0 − 20.0) = −1.67×104 J.
  2. Heat needed to melt all the ice: Qmelt = mLf = (0.050)(3.33×105) = 1.67×104 J.
  3. Compare magnitudes: |Qcool| is just slightly larger than Qmelt, so all the ice melts and there is a small leftover energy to warm the resulting 0.250 kg of water above 0 °C.
  4. Leftover heat: Qleft ≈ 1.67×104 − 1.665×104 ≈ 9.4×101 J. Then ΔT = Qleft/(mc) = 94/(0.250·4186) = 0.090 K.

Answer: All the ice melts and Tf ≈ 0.09 °C (about 0 °C).

18.5 The First Law of Thermodynamics

Statement

ΔEint = Q − W

Eint is internal energy (microscopic kinetic + potential energies within the system). W is work done by the system on its surroundings.

The first law is an energy bookkeeping rule: any change in internal energy must come from energy transferred as heat and/or work.

18.5.1 The First Law and Sign Conventions

Interpretation

Term Positive means… Common confusion
Q heat into the system Heat is not “stored”; it is transfer
W work done by the system Some courses use the opposite sign; always check
ΔEint internal energy increases Depends on state, not path

A quick sanity check: if the system expands and pushes the environment, W > 0. If you compress the system, W < 0.

18.5.2 PV Work: W = ∫P dV

Quasi-static expansion/compression

W = ∫ P dV

Graphically, W is the signed area under the curve on a P–V diagram. For approximately constant pressure, W ≈ PΔV.

Common sign traps
  • Expansion: ΔV > 0 ⇒ W > 0.
  • Compression: ΔV < 0 ⇒ W < 0.
  • No volume change: ΔV = 0 ⇒ W = 0.

18.5.3 Common Processes (Isochoric/Isothermal/Adiabatic)

Quick table (using ΔEint = Q − W)

Process Constraint Work W Heat Q First-law implication
Isochoric V constant W = 0 Q can be ± ΔEint = Q
Isothermal (ideal gas) T constant W can be ± Q matches W ΔEint = 0 ⇒ Q = W
Adiabatic Q = 0 W can be ± Q = 0 ΔEint = −W

The “isothermal (ideal gas)” row uses the fact that an ideal gas’s internal energy depends only on temperature.

18.5.4 Worked Example: Isothermal Expansion Work

1.00 mol ideal gas doubles its volume at 300 K

Given: n = 1.00 mol, T = 300 K, Vf = 2Vi.

  1. For an isothermal ideal gas, W = nRT ln(Vf/Vi).
  2. W = (1.00)(8.314)(300) ln 2 = 1.73×103 J.
  3. Isothermal ideal gas ⇒ ΔEint = 0 ⇒ Q = W.

Answer: The gas does 1.73 kJ of work; it must absorb 1.73 kJ of heat to keep T constant.

18.6 Heat Transfer Mechanisms

Three mechanisms

Conduction: energy transfer by microscopic collisions in a material (dominant in solids).

Convection: energy transfer by bulk fluid motion (natural or forced).

Radiation: energy transfer by electromagnetic waves (works through vacuum).

Rule of thumb

In real situations, multiple mechanisms act in parallel. A good first pass is to estimate each rate and see which is largest.

18.6.1 Conduction

Steady-state slab model

Pcond = kA(ΔT/L)

k is thermal conductivity (W/m·K), A is area, L is thickness, and ΔT is the temperature difference across the material.

This formula assumes a simple geometry and a linear temperature gradient. Surface convection can dominate the overall thermal resistance.

18.6.2 Convection

Newton’s cooling law (empirical)

Pconv = hA(T − Tenv)

h depends strongly on fluid speed and geometry. Natural convection (buoyancy-driven) usually has smaller h than forced convection (fan).

18.6.3 Radiation

Stefan–Boltzmann law

Prad = εσA(T4 − Tenv4)

ε is emissivity (0–1) and σ = 5.67×10−8 W/m2·K4. Use Kelvin in T4.

18.6.4 Worked Example: Conduction Through Insulation

Estimate heat-loss rate through a foam wall

Given: A = 10 m2, L = 0.10 m, k = 0.040 W/(m·K), indoor–outdoor ΔT = 25 K.

  1. Use P = kA(ΔT/L).
  2. P = (0.040)(10)(25/0.10) = 100 W.

Answer: Conduction through the insulation is about 100 W under these conditions.

Study tip: for any thermal problem, write the system boundary first, then track signs with the same convention throughout: heat into system (Q > 0) and work done by system (W > 0). For calorimetry, do the energy accounting in stages.
