26.1 Electric Current

Key ideas

Current is about net charge flow through a surface. Conduction electrons in a metal move randomly at high speeds, but without an applied field there is no net transport and thus no current.

Current measures flow rate Steady current: same i through any cross section Charge conservation at junctions

Definition

i = dq/dt
q(t) = ∫ i dt

26.1.1 Definition and Charge Conservation

Key ideas

Steady current: i is constant in time and the same for any plane that cuts through the conductor.

Junction rule: total current into a junction equals total current out (no charge pileup in steady state).

Worked Example: Charge through a point

Problem: A steady current of 2.5 A flows for 3.0 min. How much charge passes a point?

  1. q = it = (2.5 C/s)(180 s) = 450 C.

Answer: 4.5 × 102 C.

26.1.2 Current Direction Convention

Key ideas

By convention, current direction is the direction that positive charge would move. In metals, the actual carriers are electrons, so their drift is opposite the conventional current direction.

Current arrows in circuit diagrams are not vectors; they indicate the assumed direction for i in equations. If you guess “wrong”, you’ll get a negative i and the physics is still consistent.

26.2 Current Density

Key ideas

Current density J describes how current is distributed in space: it is current per unit area flowing through a surface.

Core relationships

i = ∫ J · dA
Uniform flow perpendicular to area A:   i = JA

26.2.1 J and Area Vectors

Key ideas

The area element dA points normal to the surface. The dot product J · dA automatically picks out the component of current density through that surface.

26.2.2 Drift Speed

Key ideas

Conduction electrons move randomly fast, but a weak electric field produces a tiny average drift velocity vd, which creates the net current.

Random speed ≫ drift speed More carriers → larger i for same vd vd depends on material + geometry

Useful formulas

J = nq vd
i = nqAvd (uniform wire)
Worked Example: Estimate drift speed in a copper wire

Given: i = 2.0 A in a wire of area A = 1.0 mm2, with carrier density n = 8.5 × 1028 m−3, q = e.

  1. vd = i/(nqA).
  2. A = 1.0 mm2 = 1.0 × 10−6 m2.
  3. vd ≈ 2.0 / [(8.5×1028)(1.60×10−19)(1.0×10−6)] ≈ 1.5×10−4 m/s.

Answer: Drift speed is on the order of 10−4 m/s (fractions of a mm/s).

26.3 Resistance and Resistivity

Key ideas

Resistance R is a property of a particular object (shape + material). Resistivity ρ is a material property describing how strongly the material resists current flow.

Core formulas

R = ρL/A
V = iR

26.3.1 R vs. ρ

Key ideas

Resistivity ρ: material constant (Ω·m), depends on temperature and material composition.

Resistance R: depends on geometry via L/A and on material via ρ.

Worked Example: Doubling wire length

Question: What happens to R if a wire’s length doubles (same material, same area)?

  1. R = ρL/A.
  2. If L → 2L, then R → 2R.

Answer: Resistance doubles.

26.3.2 Temperature Dependence

Key ideas

In many metals, resistivity increases approximately linearly with temperature because electron scattering increases. For semiconductors, increasing temperature can increase carrier density and decrease resistivity.

26.4 Ohm’s Law

Key ideas

A device is ohmic if R = V/i is constant (independent of V and polarity). A material is ohmic if ρ = E/J is constant (independent of applied field magnitude/direction).

26.4.1 Ohmic Devices vs. Non-Ohmic

Key ideas

Ohmic: V–i graph is a straight line through the origin; slope is R.

Non-ohmic: resistance changes with V (e.g., diodes, filament lamps, many semiconductors).

Worked Example: Interpreting a V–i graph

Question: If a device has V proportional to i, what does that say about R?

  1. Ohm’s law form: V = iR.
  2. If V ∝ i, then R is constant (the proportionality constant).

Answer: The device is ohmic with constant resistance.

26.4.2 Microscopic View (Mean Free Time τ)

Key ideas

Electrons accelerate between collisions with lattice atoms; the average time between collisions is τ. A simple model leads to a resistivity that depends on τ and carrier density n.

Key result (simple Drude model)

ρ = m / (e2 n τ)

26.5 Power

Key ideas

A potential difference V drives current i; energy is transferred at a rate (power) P. In resistors, the transferred energy becomes thermal energy.

26.5.1 Power Identities

Formulas

P = iV
Resistor: P = i2R = V2/R
Worked Example: Choosing the easiest power formula

Problem: A 12 V source drives 0.50 A through a device. Find power.

  1. Use P = iV.
  2. P = (0.50)(12) = 6.0 W.

Answer: 6.0 W.

26.5.2 Energy Flow Idea

Key idea

When charge dq moves through a potential drop V, its electric potential energy decreases by dU = dqV. In steady current i, that energy transfer rate is P = dU/dt = iV.

“Electrons move slowly” does not mean “energy moves slowly”. Circuit energy transfer is associated with fields established throughout the circuit.

Study tip: Always separate definitions (i = dq/dt, J, ρ, R) from constitutive laws (Ohm’s law, material dependence). Use units as a consistency check: A, V, Ω, W.
