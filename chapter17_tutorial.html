17.1 Speed of Sound

Big idea

Wave speed is determined by the medium (its “stiffness” and “inertia”), not by the source. For sound, the source sets the frequency, and the medium sets the speed; then wavelength follows from v = fλ.

Medium sets v Source sets f λ = v/f

17.1.1 What Controls Wave Speed in a Medium

Stiffness ÷ density

Fluids (including gases):

v = √(B/ρ)

B is the bulk modulus (how hard it is to compress), ρ is density.

Slender solids (longitudinal waves):

v = √(Y/ρ)

Y is Young’s modulus (stiffness against stretching/compression).

Ideal gas temperature dependence

v = √(γRT/M)

γ is the heat-capacity ratio, M is molar mass (kg/mol). This makes v increase roughly as √T. Humidity also matters (changes effective M).

17.1.2 Worked Example: Speed of Sound vs Temperature

Estimate v in air at 0 °C and 20 °C

Given: Use the √T scaling from v = √(γRT/M). Take v(0 °C) ≈ 331 m/s at T0 = 273.15 K.

  1. At 20 °C, T = 293.15 K, so v(20 °C) ≈ v(0 °C) √(T/T0).
  2. v ≈ 331 √(293.15/273.15) = 343 m/s.

Answer: v increases from about 331 m/s (0 °C) to about 343 m/s (20 °C).

17.2 Traveling Sound Waves

Longitudinal wave picture

Sound in a fluid is a longitudinal wave: the medium oscillates back and forth in the direction of wave travel, producing compressions and rarefactions. A traveling wave carries energy without carrying matter along overall.

17.2.1 Displacement, Pressure, and Particle Speed

Three “signals” in a sound wave

Quantity What it means Typical form (sinusoidal)
Displacement s(x,t) particle shift from equilibrium s = sm cos(kx − ωt)
Pressure variation Δp(x,t) pressure above/below ambient Δp = Δpm sin(kx − ωt)
Particle speed u(x,t) speed of the medium elements u = ∂s/∂t

The wave speed v is not the same as the particle speed u. Particles oscillate locally; the pattern (phase) travels at speed v.

17.2.2 Intensity Scales with Amplitude

Energy flow

I = P/A

Intensity I is power per area (W/m2).

For a sinusoidal sound wave in a fixed medium, intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude (pressure amplitude or displacement amplitude): doubling amplitude makes intensity about 4× larger.

17.3 Interference

Superposition

When two waves overlap in space, the net disturbance is the sum of the individual disturbances. This can produce regions of constructive interference (louder) and destructive interference (quieter).

Two coherent sources (same frequency)

Δϕ = 2π(Δr/λ)

Δr is path difference (difference in distances from the two sources to the point).

17.3.1 Path Difference and Phase Difference

Conditions

Outcome Path difference Δr Phase difference Δϕ
Constructive Δr = mλ Δϕ = 2πm
Destructive Δr = (m + 1/2)λ Δϕ = (2m + 1)π

m is any integer (0, 1, 2, …).

17.3.2 Worked Example: Two-Speaker Interference

Where is the first “quiet spot” on the centerline?

Setup: Two speakers emit the same tone (same f, in phase) and are separated by distance d. At a distant point on the perpendicular bisector (“centerline”), the path difference is ~0, so it’s loud. Move sideways until the path difference becomes λ/2 to get the first destructive interference.

  1. Compute wavelength from λ = v/f using the medium’s v.
  2. Set the geometric path difference to Δr = λ/2 and solve for the sideways position (often using small-angle approximations).

Answer: The first minimum occurs at the location where the distances differ by λ/2.

17.4 Intensity and Sound Level

Inverse-square spreading

I = P/(4πr2)   (point source, no absorption)

In real air, absorption and reflections complicate this, but inverse-square is a great first estimate.

Why use decibels?

Human hearing spans an enormous intensity range, so a logarithmic scale is convenient and matches perception more closely than a linear scale.

17.4.1 Sound Level β (Decibels)

Definition

β = 10 log10(I/I0)   dB

Reference intensity I0 = 1.0×10−12 W/m2 (approx threshold of hearing).

Intensity change Δβ Rule of thumb
I × 10 +10 dB “one decade” louder
I × 2 +3.0 dB doubling intensity ≈ +3 dB
I ÷ 2 −3.0 dB halving intensity ≈ −3 dB

17.4.2 Worked Example: Decibels and Distance

How much does sound level drop when you double distance?

Assume: point source, no absorption. Then I ∝ 1/r2.

  1. Doubling distance: r → 2r makes intensity I → I/4.
  2. Δβ = 10 log10((I/4)/I) = 10 log10(1/4) = −6.02 dB.

Answer: About a 6 dB decrease when distance doubles (in free-field conditions).

17.5 Sources of Musical Sound

Resonance creates strong tones

Musical instruments usually produce a fundamental frequency plus harmonics because they create standing waves under boundary constraints (fixed ends, open/closed pipe ends).

17.5.1 Standing Waves in Strings and Air Columns

Strings (fixed ends)

fn = n(v/2L),   n = 1,2,3,…

Here L is the string length and v is the wave speed on the string (often v = √(T/μ), tension over linear density).

Air columns

“Open” ends are displacement antinodes (pressure nodes). “Closed” ends are displacement nodes (pressure antinodes).

17.5.2 Harmonics and Resonance Conditions

Open–open pipe (or open–open effective)

fn = n(v/2L),   n = 1,2,3,…

Open–closed pipe

fn = n(v/4L),   n = 1,3,5,… (odd only)

Only odd harmonics fit because one end must be a node and the other an antinode.

17.6 Beats

Amplitude modulation from two close frequencies

fbeat = |f1 − f2|

Beats are not a separate frequency “in the air”; they are the envelope pattern from superposition of two similar-frequency tones.

17.6.1 Beat Frequency and Tuning

Use beats to tune: make the beating stop
  1. Play a reference tone (known f) and your instrument tone (unknown f).
  2. Listen for beats and measure/estimate fbeat.
  3. Adjust until fbeat → 0 (frequencies match).

17.7 The Doppler Effect

Idea

When there is relative motion along the line of sight, the received frequency differs from the emitted frequency. In a medium (sound), source motion and observer motion contribute differently.

17.7.1 Moving Source and/or Observer

General form (sound in still air)

f' = f (v ± vo)/(v ∓ vs)

Choose +vo when the observer moves toward the source; choose −vs in the denominator when the source moves toward the observer (making wavefronts closer together).

Keep directions straight: treat speeds along the line connecting source and observer. A quick check: approaching should increase f'.

17.7.2 Worked Example: Siren Passes By

Compute f' just before and after passing

Given: siren frequency f, sound speed v, source speed vs (observer stationary).

  1. Approaching: f'app = f · v/(v − vs).
  2. Receding: f'rec = f · v/(v + vs).
  3. The “jump” you hear is from the sign change in the denominator at the pass-by.

Answer: Approaching gives a higher f', receding gives a lower f'.

17.8 Supersonic Speeds, Shock Waves

When the source outruns its waves

If a source moves faster than the wave speed in the medium (supersonic), the wavefronts pile up into a shock wave. The shock forms a cone (in 3D) behind the source; observers hear a sonic boom when the shock passes.

Mach number Mach cone Sonic boom

17.8.1 Mach Number and Mach Cone

Definitions

M = vs/v
sin θ = v/vs = 1/M

θ is the half-angle of the Mach cone; larger M means a narrower cone.

Study tip: for sound problems, write the three “layers” explicitly: (1) wave kinematics (v = fλ), (2) energy measures (I, β in dB), and (3) geometry (path differences for interference; 1/r2 spreading). Keep sign conventions consistent in Doppler problems.
