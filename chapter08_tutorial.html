8.1 Potential Energy

Potential energy U is defined for conservative forces so that work depends only on endpoints. The change in potential energy is the negative of the work done by the conservative force.

ΔU = −Wcons

Common examples

Ug = mgy (near Earth)
Us = (1/2)kx2 (spring)

8.2 Conservation of Mechanical Energy

If only conservative forces do work within a system, mechanical energy is conserved: kinetic energy and potential energy trade off, but their sum stays constant.

K + U = constant

If friction or a motor does work, mechanical energy is not conserved; use the work–energy form instead.

8.3 Reading a Potential Energy Curve

A plot of U(x) tells you allowed motion and forces. Where total energy E intersects U(x) are turning points (K = 0). The slope gives the force: F = −dU/dx.

K(x) = E − U(x)
F(x) = −dU/dx

8.4 Work Done on a System by an External Force

If an external agent does work on the system, it changes the system’s mechanical energy. This is the clean way to include friction or a push/pull without tracking microscopic details.

Wext = Δ(K + U)
Worked example: push a block up a ramp

You can treat the system as “block + Earth.” Then ΔU = mgΔy. Any extra energy beyond mgΔy must come from external work (and friction can remove mechanical energy as negative work).

8.5 Conservation of Energy

Energy can change form (mechanical ↔ thermal ↔ chemical, etc.) but total energy is conserved. If mechanical energy decreases because of friction, it typically appears as thermal energy.

ΔEtotal = 0

For a chosen system, track energy transfers across the boundary as work/heat or other mechanisms.

Study tip: start by choosing the system. If only conservative forces act inside the system, use K + U conservation. If not, use Wext = Δ(K + U).
