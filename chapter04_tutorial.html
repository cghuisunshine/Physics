4.1 Position and Displacement

In 2D/3D, position is a vector r(t). Displacement is Δr = rf − ri. Vector thinking (components) keeps direction explicit.

Δr = rf − ri

4.2 Average Velocity and Instantaneous Velocity

Velocity is the rate of change of position. Average velocity uses displacement over time; instantaneous velocity is the derivative.

v̄ = Δr/Δt
v = dr/dt

4.3 Average Acceleration and Instantaneous Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity (vector). Even if speed is constant, acceleration can be nonzero when direction changes (circular motion).

ā = Δv/Δt
a = dv/dt = d2r/dt2

4.4 Projectile Motion

With no air resistance, horizontal motion has constant velocity while vertical motion has constant acceleration (−g). Treat x and y independently.

Component equations

x(t) = x0 + v0xt
y(t) = y0 + v0yt − (1/2)gt2
Tip: solve time from one axis, plug into the other

Find t from y(t) (or from x(t) if range is given), then substitute into x(t) or y(t).

4.5 Uniform Circular Motion

Uniform circular motion has constant speed but changing direction. Acceleration points toward the center.

ac = v2/r = ω2r

4.6 Relative Motion in One Dimension

Relative velocity is the rate of change of one object’s position as seen from another. In 1D, velocities add/subtract with signs.

vA/B = vA − vB

4.7 Relative Motion in Two Dimensions

In 2D, relative motion is vector subtraction. Many “boat in a river” and “airplane in wind” problems are just vector addition.

vA/C = vA/B + vB/C

Study tip: in 2D, write everything in components early. For projectiles, separate x and y. For relative motion, write a velocity-addition equation and draw the vector triangle.
