33.1 Electromagnetic Waves

Key ideas

An electromagnetic wave is a traveling disturbance of oscillating electric and magnetic fields. The fields are mutually perpendicular and also perpendicular to the direction of propagation.

E ⟂ B Both ⟂ propagation Speed in vacuum is c

Wave model

E = Em sin(kx − ωt)     B = Bm sin(kx − ωt)

Same phase, same frequency, traveling in +x direction.

33.1.1 Wave Relations (c, f, λ)

Core relations

c = fλ     ω = 2πf     k = 2π/λ

Speed from constants

c = 1/√(μ0ε0)
Worked Example: Convert frequency to wavelength

Given: FM radio at f = 100 MHz.

  1. λ = c/f = (3.00×108 m/s)/(1.00×108 s−1).
  2. λ = 3.00 m.

Answer: λ ≈ 3.0 m.

33.1.2 Field Amplitudes (E and B)

Amplitude relation

E/B = c   ⇒   Em/Bm = c
Worked Example: Magnetic amplitude from electric amplitude

Given: Em = 120 V/m (in vacuum).

  1. Bm = Em/c = 120/(3.00×108) T.
  2. Bm = 4.0×10−7 T.

Answer: Bm = 4.0×10−7 T.

33.2 Energy Transport and the Poynting Vector

Key ideas

EM waves carry energy and momentum. The energy flow (power per area) is described by the Poynting vector S.

Poynting vector (magnitude)

S = (1/μ0)EB

Direction is along propagation: S = (1/μ0) E × B.

33.2.1 Intensity and Averages

Average intensity (sinusoidal wave)

I = ⟨S⟩ = (1/2)(1/μ0)EmBm = (1/2)ε0cEm2
Worked Example: Intensity from E amplitude

Given: Em = 200 V/m in vacuum.

  1. I = ½ ε0cEm2 = 0.5(8.85×10−12)(3.00×108)(2002).
  2. I ≈ 53 W/m2.

Answer: I ≈ 5.3×101 W/m2.

33.3 Radiation Pressure

Key idea

Because EM waves carry momentum, they exert pressure on surfaces when absorbed or reflected. Pressure depends on the intensity (average energy flow).

33.3.1 Pressure on Absorbing vs Reflecting Surfaces

Formulas (normal incidence)

pabsorb = I/c
preflect = 2I/c
Worked Example: Radiation pressure

Given: Sunlight intensity I = 1.0 kW/m2 on a perfectly reflecting surface.

  1. p = 2I/c = 2(1000)/(3.00×108) Pa.
  2. p = 6.7×10−6 Pa.

Answer: p ≈ 6.7 μPa.

33.4 Polarization

Key ideas

Polarization describes the direction of the electric field oscillation in a transverse wave. Many sources produce unpolarized light (random field directions), but polarizers can transmit only a chosen component.

33.4.1 Linear Polarization and Polarizers

Polarizer as component selector

An ideal linear polarizer transmits the component of E along its transmission axis and blocks the perpendicular component. Light emerging from the polarizer is linearly polarized.

33.4.2 Malus’ Law

Two polarizers at angle θ

I = I0 cos2θ
Worked Example: Crossed polarizers

Given: Linearly polarized light with intensity I0 passes a second polarizer whose axis is at θ = 60°.

  1. I = I0cos260° = I0(0.5)2 = 0.25 I0.

Answer: Transmitted intensity is 25% of I0.

33.5 Reflection and Refraction

Key ideas

At an interface between media, part of a wave reflects and part transmits (refracts). The ray model gives the familiar laws of reflection and refraction (Snell’s law).

Index and speed

n = c/v

Higher n means lower wave speed v in the medium.

33.5.1 Snell’s Law

Refraction relation

n1 sinθ1 = n2 sinθ2
Worked Example: Refracted angle

Given: Light goes from air (n ≈ 1.00) into glass (n = 1.50) at θ1 = 30°.

  1. sinθ2 = (n1/n2)sinθ1 = (1.00/1.50)(0.5) = 0.333.
  2. θ2 = sin−1(0.333) ≈ 19.5°.

Answer: θ2 ≈ 19.5°.

33.5.2 Index and Speed

Speed relationship

v = c/n

When entering a higher-index medium, the wave slows and bends toward the normal (θ decreases). Frequency stays the same; wavelength changes because v = fλ.

33.6 Total Internal Reflection

Key idea

If light travels from higher index to lower index (n1 > n2), the refracted angle grows with incidence angle. Beyond a critical angle, no refracted ray exists: all energy reflects.

33.6.1 Critical Angle

Condition for total internal reflection

sinθc = n2/n1    (n1 > n2)
Worked Example: Critical angle for glass–air

Given: n1 = 1.50 (glass), n2 = 1.00 (air).

  1. sinθc = 1.00/1.50 = 0.667.
  2. θc = sin−1(0.667) ≈ 41.8°.

Answer: θc ≈ 42°.

33.7 Polarization by Reflection

Key idea

Reflection from a dielectric surface can polarize light. At a special incidence angle (Brewster angle), the reflected light is completely linearly polarized.

33.7.1 Brewster Angle

Formula

tanθB = n2/n1

At θB, the reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular (θr + θt = 90°).

Worked Example: Brewster angle (air–glass)

Given: n1 = 1.00 (air), n2 = 1.50 (glass).

  1. θB = tan−1(1.50) = 56.3°.

Answer: θB ≈ 56°.

Study tip: keep a small “toolbox” for wave problems: c = fλ, ω = 2πf, E/B = c, and the optics trio n = c/v, n1sinθ1 = n2sinθ2, sinθc = n2/n1.
