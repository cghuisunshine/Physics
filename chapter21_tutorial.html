21.1 Coulomb’s Law

Key ideas

Two point charges exert forces on each other along the line joining them. Like charges repel; unlike charges attract. The magnitude falls off as 1/r2, just like gravity.

Force along the line Inverse-square Attract vs repel

Magnitude

F = k|q1q2|/r2

k = 1/(4πϵ0) ≈ 8.99×109 N·m2/C2.

21.1.1 Vector Form and Superposition

Vector form

F12 = (1/4πϵ01q2/r2) r12

r12 is the unit vector pointing from charge 1 to charge 2. The sign of q1q2 determines whether the force points along +r12 (repulsion) or −r12 (attraction).

Superposition

If multiple charges act on a charge q, the net force is the vector sum: Fnet = ΣFi. Compute each pairwise Coulomb force (with directions), then add components.

Don’t “add magnitudes” unless forces are collinear. In general, resolve into x/y components and add vectors.

21.1.2 Worked Example: Force Between Two Charges

Find the magnitude and direction of the force

Given: q1 = +2.0 μC, q2 = −5.0 μC, separation r = 0.30 m.

  1. Magnitude: F = k|q1q2|/r2 = (8.99×109)(|2.0×10−6·5.0×10−6|)/(0.30)2.
  2. Compute: |q1q2| = 1.0×10−11 C2, so F = (8.99×109)(1.0×10−11)/0.09 ≈ 1.00 N.
  3. Direction: q1q2 is negative → attraction. Each force points toward the other charge.

Answer: F ≈ 1.0 N, attractive (toward the other charge along the line joining them).

21.1.3 Worked Example: Net Force from Two Charges (2D)

Add Coulomb forces as vectors

Setup: A test charge q0 = +1.0 μC is at the origin. A charge qx = −2.0 μC is at (0.30 m, 0). A charge qy = +3.0 μC is at (0, 0.40 m).

  1. Force from qx: magnitude Fx = k|q0qx|/(0.30)2 = (8.99×109)(2.0×10−12)/0.09 ≈ 0.200 N, direction +x (attraction).
  2. Force from qy: magnitude Fy = k|q0qy|/(0.40)2 = (8.99×109)(3.0×10−12)/0.16 ≈ 0.169 N, direction −y (repulsion).
  3. Net components: Fnet,x = +0.200 N, Fnet,y = −0.169 N.
  4. Magnitude: |Fnet| = √(0.2002 + 0.1692) ≈ 0.262 N.
  5. Direction: θ = tan−1(|Fnet,y|/Fnet,x) ≈ 40° below the +x axis.

Answer: Fnet ≈ (0.200i − 0.169j) N, magnitude ≈ 0.262 N at ~40° below +x.

21.2 Charge Is Quantized

Key ideas

Electric charge comes in discrete chunks. The smallest free charge magnitude is the elementary charge e. Any isolated object’s net charge is an integer multiple of e.

q = ne e = 1.602×10−19 C Integers only

Quantization

q = n e, with n = 0, ±1, ±2, …

Electrons carry −e; protons carry +e.

21.2.1 q = ne and Counting Electrons

How many excess electrons correspond to −4.8 nC?

Given: q = −4.8 nC = −4.8×10−9 C, e = 1.602×10−19 C.

  1. n = q/e = (−4.8×10−9)/(−1.602×10−19) = 3.0×1010.

Answer: About 3×1010 excess electrons.

Quantization is usually invisible in macroscopic charges because even 1 nC corresponds to ~6×109 electrons.

21.3 Charge Is Conserved

Key ideas

The total electric charge of an isolated system remains constant. Charge can move from one object to another, but the net sum does not change. This is as fundamental as conservation of energy and momentum.

21.3.1 Conservation in Contact and Separation

Touching conductors (simple model)

If two identical conducting spheres touch, charge redistributes until their potentials are equal. For identical spheres, that means they share charge equally: each ends with (q1 + q2)/2.

Worked Example: Two spheres share charge

Given: Sphere A has +6 nC, sphere B has −2 nC. They touch and are separated (assume identical spheres).

  1. Total charge: qtot = +6 nC + (−2 nC) = +4 nC (conserved).
  2. Identical sharing: each ends with qf = qtot/2 = +2 nC.

Answer: After contact, each sphere has +2 nC; total remains +4 nC.

Study tip: for electrostatics, write a “three-step recipe”: (1) draw positions and signs, (2) write each Coulomb force with a unit vector, (3) add components. If you get stuck, check units: N = (N·m2/C2)·C2/m2.
