27.1 Single-Loop Circuits

Key ideas

A circuit needs a “charge pump” to maintain a potential difference: an emf source does work on charge carriers. In a steady DC circuit, conservation of energy and charge give you two powerful rules: the loop rule and the junction rule.

Loop rule: ΣΔV = 0 around any closed loop Junction rule: Σiin = Σiout Sign conventions matter (but are consistent)

Core definitions

ε = dW/dq (emf = work per unit charge)
Resistor drop: V = iR

27.1.1 Emf and Potential Changes

Key ideas

Emf device: raises electric potential by doing work on charges (chemical, mechanical, etc.).

Resistor: converts electrical energy to thermal energy; potential drops in the direction of current.

Worked Example: Energy bookkeeping

Question: If charge dq passes through an emf ε and then through a resistor with drop iR, what must be true in a single loop?

  1. Loop rule: the net change in potential around the loop is zero.
  2. So the rise +ε must be balanced by drops (including iR and any internal drops).

Takeaway: The emf supplies energy that is dissipated in resistances.

27.1.2 Loop Rule Sign Conventions

Rules of thumb

  • Resistor: traverse in direction of current ⇒ ΔV = −iR; opposite direction ⇒ ΔV = +iR.
  • Ideal emf: traverse in direction of emf arrow (− to + terminal) ⇒ ΔV = +ε; opposite ⇒ ΔV = −ε.
Worked Example: One-loop with one battery and one resistor

Given: ε = 9.0 V, R = 3.0 Ω.

  1. Choose loop direction as current direction.
  2. Loop rule: +ε − iR = 0 ⇒ i = ε/R = 3.0 A.

Answer: i = 3.0 A.

You can pick any traversal direction and any assumed current direction. If your assumption is opposite reality, your solved current will be negative (which is fine).

27.1.3 Real Battery (Internal Resistance)

Key ideas

A real battery behaves like an ideal emf ε in series with an internal resistance r. The terminal voltage depends on the current direction.

Useful results

Delivering power (current out of + terminal):   Vterm = ε − ir
Charging (current into + terminal):   Vterm = ε + ir
Internal dissipation: Pr = i2r
Worked Example: Terminal voltage under load

Given: ε = 12.0 V, r = 0.50 Ω, current i = 4.0 A delivered to the circuit.

  1. Vterm = ε − ir = 12.0 − (4.0)(0.50) = 10.0 V.
  2. Pr = i2r = 16(0.50) = 8.0 W (lost inside battery).

Answer: Terminal voltage 10.0 V; 8 W dissipated internally.

27.1.4 Series Resistors

Key ideas

In a single loop, resistors in series carry the same current. The equivalent resistance is the sum.

Results

Req = R1 + R2 + …
Vtotal = V1 + V2 + … (same i)
Worked Example: Voltage divider (two resistors)

Given: ε = 10 V applied to R1 = 2 Ω and R2 = 3 Ω in series. Find V across R2.

  1. i = ε/(R1+R2) = 10/5 = 2 A.
  2. V2 = iR2 = (2)(3) = 6 V.

Answer: V across R2 is 6 V.

27.1.5 Grounding Idea

Key ideas

“Ground” is a chosen reference point defined to be 0 V. In an ideal DC circuit analysis, grounding sets a reference potential but does not by itself change the currents.

In practice, grounding can matter for safety, shielding, and unintended current paths; the textbook idealization focuses on the reference-potential role.

27.2 Multiloop Circuits

Key ideas

For multiloop circuits, use the junction rule to express charge conservation at nodes, and the loop rule to express energy conservation around loops. You’ll usually solve a system of linear equations for unknown currents.

27.2.1 Kirchhoff Strategy

Workflow

  1. Label junctions and assign current directions (arbitrary but consistent).
  2. Write junction equations (Σiin = Σiout).
  3. Pick independent loops and write loop equations (ΣΔV = 0).
  4. Solve the resulting linear system; interpret any negative currents as opposite to your assumed direction.
Worked Example (Structure): Two-loop system

Idea: With two loops sharing a resistor, choose loop currents i1 and i2. The shared resistor sees current (i1 − i2).

  1. Write loop 1: +ε1 − i1R1 − (i1 − i2)Rs = 0.
  2. Write loop 2: +ε2 − i2R2 − (i2 − i1)Rs = 0.
  3. Solve for i1, i2, then compute branch currents.

Tip: Shared-element signs are the common source of mistakes—use (i1 − i2) consistently.

27.2.2 Loop-Current Method

Key ideas

Loop currents simplify bookkeeping: every element belongs to one loop (unique) or is shared. You write one loop equation per loop current, then back out branch currents.

Worked Example: Quick consistency check

Check: After solving, verify power balance (energy conservation): total power supplied by sources equals total power dissipated by resistors.

Σ(iε) = Σ(i2R)

27.3 The Ammeter and the Voltmeter

Key ideas

Instruments are part of the circuit. An ammeter measures current and must be placed in series; a voltmeter measures potential difference and must be placed in parallel.

27.3.1 Ideal vs. Real Meters

Ideal targets

Ideal ammeter: RA = 0 (so it doesn’t change the current).

Real ammeters have small but nonzero resistance.

Ideal voltmeter: RV → ∞ (so it draws negligible current).

Real voltmeters have large but finite resistance.

Mis-wiring can be destructive: placing an ammeter in parallel can short a source; placing a voltmeter in series can open the circuit.

27.3.2 Meter Loading

Key idea

A finite voltmeter resistance draws current, altering the node voltages you are trying to measure. This is called loading.

Worked Example: Voltmeter loading on a divider

Setup: A divider R1–R2 is measured across R2 using a voltmeter of resistance RV.

  1. The meter is in parallel with R2, so the effective lower resistance becomes R2,eff = (R2RV)/(R2+RV).
  2. This changes the divider ratio and the measured voltage drops.

Rule of thumb: If RV ≫ R2, loading is negligible.

27.4 RC Circuits

Key ideas

RC circuits have time-varying currents and voltages. The capacitor’s voltage cannot change instantly because q and VC are linked by q = CVC. The time scale is the time constant τ = RC.

27.4.1 Time Constant

Definition

τ = RC

After 1τ: charging reaches about 63% of final value; discharging drops to about 37% of initial value.

27.4.2 Charging (Series RC with Battery)

Standard results

q(t) = Cε(1 − e−t/RC)
VC(t) = ε(1 − e−t/RC)
i(t) = (ε/R)e−t/RC
Worked Example: Time to reach 90% charge

Question: When does q(t) = 0.90(Cε)?

  1. 0.90 = 1 − e−t/RC ⇒ e−t/RC = 0.10.
  2. t = −RC ln(0.10) = 2.30RC = 2.30τ.

Answer: About 2.3 time constants.

27.4.3 Discharging (No Battery)

Standard results

q(t) = q0e−t/RC
VC(t) = V0e−t/RC
i(t) = −(q0/RC)e−t/RC = −(V0/R)e−t/RC
Worked Example: Half-life for discharge

Question: When does q(t) = q0/2?

  1. 1/2 = e−t/RC.
  2. t = RC ln2 ≈ 0.693RC = 0.693τ.

Answer: Half-life is 0.693τ.

The negative sign in i(t) comes from a chosen reference current direction; it indicates current flows opposite the charging direction during discharge.

Study tip: In DC resistive circuits, write loop/junction equations. In RC circuits, remember that q and VC are linked (q = CVC) and that exponentials always involve t/RC.
