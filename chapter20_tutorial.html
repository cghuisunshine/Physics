20.1 Entropy

Key ideas

Entropy S is a state function that tracks the dispersal of energy and the “direction” of real processes. The second law says the total entropy of an isolated system never decreases.

State function Direction of processes ΔSuniv ≥ 0

Second law

ΔSuniverse = ΔSsystem + ΔSsurroundings ≥ 0

Equality holds for an ideal reversible process; strict > 0 for irreversible processes.

20.1.1 Definition for Reversible Heat

Definition

dS = dQrev/T

You can compute ΔS using any convenient reversible path connecting the same initial and final states (even if the real process is irreversible).

Common special cases (quick)
ΔS (ideal gas, isothermal) = nR ln(Vf/Vi) = nR ln(Pi/Pf)
ΔS (heating, constant T) = Q/T
ΔS (warming a substance) = ∫(mc dT)/T = mc ln(Tf/Ti) (if c constant)

20.1.2 Worked Example: Isothermal Ideal-Gas Expansion

Compute entropy change for doubling the volume

Given: 1.00 mol ideal gas expands isothermally from Vi = 10.0 L to Vf = 20.0 L at T = 300 K.

  1. Use ΔS = nR ln(Vf/Vi).
  2. ΔS = (1.00)(8.314 J/mol·K) ln(2) = 5.76 J/K.

Answer: The gas entropy increases by about 5.8 J/K.

20.2 Entropy in the Real World: Engines

Key ideas

A heat engine absorbs heat QH from a hot reservoir (TH), does work W, and expels heat QC to a cold reservoir (TC). The second law limits efficiency: no engine can be more efficient than a reversible (Carnot) engine operating between the same temperatures.

20.2.1 Carnot Efficiency and ΔS

Efficiency and entropy

e = W/QH = 1 − QC/QH
For a reversible engine: QC/QH = TC/TH
Carnot (maximum) efficiency: emax = 1 − TC/TH

A reversible engine produces zero net entropy change per cycle (ΔSuniverse = 0). Real engines generate entropy (ΔSuniverse > 0), which reduces efficiency.

20.2.2 Worked Example: Maximum Efficiency

Find Carnot efficiency between 600 K and 300 K

Given: TH = 600 K, TC = 300 K.

  1. emax = 1 − TC/TH = 1 − 300/600 = 0.50.

Answer: The best possible efficiency is 50%.

20.3 Refrigerators and Real Engines

Key ideas

A refrigerator (or heat pump) uses work input W to move heat from a cold reservoir to a hot one. Performance is measured by a coefficient of performance (COP), not efficiency.

20.3.1 Coefficient of Performance (COP)

Definitions

KR = QC/W (refrigerator)
KHP = QH/W (heat pump)
For an ideal (Carnot) refrigerator: KR,max = TC/(TH − TC)

Cooling gets harder as TC approaches TH: the required work per unit heat removed grows.

20.3.2 Worked Example: Ideal Refrigerator COP

Compute COP for a fridge at 270 K dumping heat to 295 K

Given: TC = 270 K, TH = 295 K.

  1. KR,max = TC/(TH − TC) = 270/(295 − 270) = 10.8.

Answer: The maximum possible COP is about 11 (real devices are lower).

20.4 A Statistical View of Entropy

Key ideas

The second law emerges from probability. Systems evolve toward macrostates with the greatest number of compatible microstates (largest multiplicity Ω). Entropy is a measure of that multiplicity.

20.4.1 Microstates and S = k ln Ω

Boltzmann relation

S = k ln Ω

k is Boltzmann’s constant, 1.38×10−23 J/K.

If a macrostate’s multiplicity increases by a factor, entropy increases by k ln(factor). Because Ω is typically enormous, entropy changes are macroscopic even though k is tiny.

20.4.2 Worked Example: Entropy Change from Multiplicity

Compute ΔS when multiplicity increases by 1000×

Given: Ωf = 1000 Ωi.

  1. ΔS = k ln(Ωf/Ωi) = k ln(1000) = k(6.907).
  2. ΔS = (1.38×10−23)(6.907) = 9.5×10−23 J/K.

Answer: ΔS ≈ 9.5×10−23 J/K for a single microscopic system; for macroscopic systems Ω ratios are vastly larger.

Study tip: for entropy problems, identify the system, pick a reversible path to compute ΔS, and remember the second-law test: if you compute ΔSuniverse < 0 for a proposed process, something is impossible or a sign is wrong.
