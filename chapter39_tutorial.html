39.1 Energies of a Trapped Electron

Key ideas

In quantum mechanics, a particle confined to a region has discrete allowed energies. A classic model is the 1D infinite square well: the particle is free inside (potential ≈ 0) but cannot exist outside (potential → ∞).

E is quantized Confinement raises E Smaller L → bigger spacing

Scaling

For a given state, energy scales like E ∝ 1/(mL2). Light particles in small traps have widely spaced energy levels (quantum dots, nanoparticles).

39.1.1 Energy Spectrum (Infinite Well)

Energy levels

En = n2h2/(8mL2) = n2π2ħ2/(2mL2), n = 1, 2, 3, …

There is no n = 0 state. Even the lowest state has nonzero energy (a “zero-point” consequence of confinement and the uncertainty principle).

39.1.2 Worked Example: Electron in a 1.0 nm Trap

Compute the first two energy levels in an infinite well

Given: Electron of mass m = 9.11×10−31 kg in a 1D infinite well of width L = 1.0 nm.

  1. Use E1 = h2/(8mL2).
  2. E1 = (6.63×10−34)2 / (8·9.11×10−31·(1.0×10−9)2) = 6.02×10−20 J ≈ 0.376 eV.
  3. E2 = 4E1 ≈ 1.50 eV.

Answer: E1 ≈ 0.38 eV, E2 ≈ 1.5 eV (and En ∝ n2).

39.2 Wave Functions of a Trapped Electron

Key ideas

A bound particle is described by a wavefunction ψ. The probability density of position is |ψ|2. For an infinite well, the allowed states are standing waves that fit the boundary condition ψ = 0 at the walls.

39.2.1 Wavefunction, Nodes, Normalization

Infinite well eigenfunctions

ψn(x) = √(2/L) sin(nπx/L), for 0 < x < L (and ψ = 0 outside)

Nodes

State n has (n − 1) interior nodes where ψ = 0. Higher n oscillates more rapidly and corresponds to higher energy.

Normalization

The coefficient √(2/L) enforces ∫0L|ψ|2dx = 1, so total probability is 1.

The wavefunction ψ can be negative, but |ψ|2 is always nonnegative. “Large ψ” means “high probability density,” not “large energy.”

39.2.2 Worked Example: Probability in the Middle Third

Find P(L/3 ≤ x ≤ 2L/3) for the ground state in an infinite well

Given: ψ1(x) = √(2/L) sin(πx/L).

  1. Probability: P = ∫L/32L/3|ψ1|2 dx = (2/L)∫L/32L/3sin2(πx/L) dx.
  2. Use ∫sin2(kx)dx = x/2 − sin(2kx)/(4k), with k = π/L.
  3. Result: P = 1/3 + √3/(2π) ≈ 0.609.

Answer: About 0.61 of the time the electron is in the middle third (ground state peaks near the center).

39.3 An Electron in a Finite Well

Key ideas

Real traps are not infinite: outside the well, the potential has a finite height V0. Bound states still exist, but the wavefunction does not drop to zero at the boundary; it “leaks” into the classically forbidden region as an exponentially decaying tail.

39.3.1 Evanescent Decay (Penetration Depth)

Exponential tail (for E < V0)

ψ ∝ e−κx, κ = √(2m(V0 − E))/ħ
penetration depth ≈ 1/κ
Worked Example: How far does the wavefunction leak?

Given: Electron with V0 − E = 1.0 eV (outside barrier).

  1. κ = √(2m(V0 − E))/ħ ≈ √(2·9.11×10−31·1.60×10−19)/(1.05×10−34) = 5.1×109 m−1.
  2. 1/κ ≈ 2.0×10−10 m = 0.20 nm.

Answer: The amplitude typically falls by a factor e over ~0.2 nm in this barrier.

39.4 Two- and Three-Dimensional Electron Traps

Key ideas

In 2D and 3D traps (rectangular boxes), motion separates by direction. Energies add, producing rich level patterns and degeneracy (different quantum-number sets can have the same energy).

39.4.1 2D/3D Box Energies and Degeneracy

Rectangular trap

E = (h2/8m)(nx2/Lx2 + ny2/Ly2 + nz2/Lz2)

For 2D, drop the z term. Each n is a positive integer (1, 2, 3, …).

Example (square 2D box) nx, ny Relative energy ∝ nx2 + ny2
Ground state (1, 1) 2
First excited “pair” (degenerate) (1, 2) and (2, 1) 5
Next state (2, 2) 8

Degeneracy is common when the geometry is symmetric (same side lengths). Breaking symmetry (Lx ≠ Ly) typically splits degenerate levels.

39.5 The Hydrogen Atom

Key ideas

The hydrogen atom is an electron bound by a Coulomb potential. Solving Schrödinger’s equation yields energy levels that depend only on a principal quantum number n, plus a structured set of wavefunctions (“orbitals”) labeled by additional quantum numbers.

39.5.1 Energy Levels and Quantum Numbers

Energy and size scales

En = −13.6 eV / n2, n = 1, 2, 3, …
a0 = 4πϵ0ħ2/(me2) = 0.529 Å

Quantum numbers (hydrogen)

Name Symbol Allowed values Physical meaning (quick)
Principal n 1, 2, 3, … Energy level (and typical radius scale)
Orbital angular momentum ℓ 0 … (n − 1) Orbital “shape” (s, p, d, …)
Magnetic mℓ −ℓ … +ℓ Orientation component (degeneracy)

The electron is not a little planet. Orbitals are probability distributions. When you see “radius,” read it as “typical scale” (e.g., where |ψ|2 is large), not a rigid path.

39.5.2 Worked Example: Hα Wavelength (n = 3 → 2)

Find the photon wavelength emitted in the transition n = 3 to n = 2

Given: Hydrogen energies En = −13.6 eV/n2, and hc ≈ 1240 eV·nm.

  1. ΔE = 13.6 eV(1/22 − 1/32) = 13.6(1/4 − 1/9) = 13.6(5/36) = 1.89 eV.
  2. λ = hc/ΔE ≈ (1240 eV·nm)/(1.89 eV) = 656 nm.

Answer: λ ≈ 656 nm (red Hα line in the Balmer series).

Study tip: in confinement problems, separate the “geometry” (L, dimensionality, boundary conditions) from the “quantum labels” (n, ℓ, m). Most calculations reduce to writing the right eigenvalue formula, converting units, and interpreting |ψ|2 as probability.
