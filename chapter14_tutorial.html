14.1 Fluids, Density, and Pressure

Fluids (liquids and gases) flow and exert pressure normal to any surface. Many results come from balancing forces on small fluid elements and applying energy conservation to flow.

ρ = m/V P = F/A Pressure acts normal

Units

1 Pa = 1 N/m2
1 atm = 1.013×105 Pa

14.2 Fluids at Rest

In a static fluid, pressure increases with depth because of the weight of the fluid above. At the same depth in a connected fluid, pressure is the same.

Hydrostatic pressure

P = P0 + ρgh

h is depth below the surface where pressure is P0.

14.3 Measuring Pressure

Manometers and barometers convert pressure differences into height differences of a fluid column. It’s a direct application of P = P0 + ρgh.

Barometer idea

Patm = ρgh

For mercury, a “760 mmHg” column corresponds to 1 atm at standard conditions.

14.4 Pascal’s Principle

A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished throughout the fluid. This enables hydraulic lifts.

Hydraulic relation

F1/A1 = F2/A2
Worked example: hydraulic lift

If A2 = 20A1, then F2 = 20F1. The tradeoff is distance: the small piston moves 20× farther.

14.5 Archimedes’ Principle

Buoyant force arises because pressure increases with depth. The net upward force equals the weight of displaced fluid, regardless of the object’s shape.

Buoyant force

Fb = ρfluid g Vdisp

If the object floats, Fb = weight of object ⇒ fraction submerged depends on densities.

14.6 The Equation of Continuity

For steady, incompressible flow, mass conservation implies the volume flow rate is constant along a streamline: narrow sections have higher flow speed.

Continuity (incompressible)

A1v1 = A2v2

Also written as Q = Av (volume flow rate).

14.7 Bernoulli’s Equation

Bernoulli’s equation is energy conservation along a streamline for ideal flow (incompressible, nonviscous, steady). It ties together pressure, flow speed, and height.

Bernoulli (along a streamline)

P + (1/2)ρv2 + ρgy = constant

If viscosity, turbulence, or pumps are important, Bernoulli needs extra loss/gain terms.

Study tip: in fluids, pick the right conservation law: static problems → force balance (hydrostatics); flow problems → mass conservation (continuity) and energy conservation (Bernoulli).
