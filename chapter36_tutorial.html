36.1 Single-Slit Diffraction

Key ideas

When light passes through an aperture comparable to its wavelength, it spreads and interferes with itself, producing a diffraction pattern: a bright central maximum plus weaker side maxima separated by dark minima.

Aperture size ~ λ Central maximum is widest Minima are predictable

Minima condition

a sinθ = mλ    (m = 1, 2, 3, …)

a is slit width; θ is angle from central axis to the mth minimum.

36.1.1 Minima Condition

Why it works (pairing idea)

Divide the slit into m pairs of zones. At angles where the path difference across each paired zone equals λ/2, contributions cancel pairwise, producing destructive interference (a dark fringe).

Worked Example: First minimum angle

Given: a = 0.20 mm, λ = 600 nm.

  1. First minimum: a sinθ = λ ⇒ sinθ = λ/a = (6.0×10−7)/(2.0×10−4) = 3.0×10−3.
  2. θ ≈ 3.0×10−3 rad = 0.172°.

Answer: θ ≈ 0.17° for the first minimum.

36.1.2 Width of the Central Maximum

Angular width (between first minima)

Δθ ≈ 2λ/a    (small angles)

Screen width at distance L

w ≈ 2Lλ/a
Worked Example: Central maximum width on a screen

Given: L = 2.0 m, a = 0.30 mm, λ = 500 nm.

  1. w ≈ 2Lλ/a = 2(2.0)(5.0×10−7)/(3.0×10−4).
  2. w = 6.7×10−3 m = 6.7 mm.

Answer: Central maximum width ≈ 6.7 mm.

36.2 Intensity in Single-Slit Diffraction

Key idea

The full pattern brightness is described by an intensity function that depends on angle through a parameter β. Minima occur at β = mπ, matching the simple condition a sinθ = mλ.

36.2.1 Intensity vs Angle

Define β

β = (πa/λ) sinθ

Intensity pattern

I(θ) = I0(sinβ/β)2

I0 is the central maximum intensity (θ = 0). Side maxima fall off rapidly: diffraction is strongly concentrated near θ = 0.

36.3 Diffraction by a Circular Aperture

Key ideas

A circular aperture (like a camera lens or telescope) produces an Airy pattern: a bright central disk (Airy disk) surrounded by rings. The finite size of this disk limits resolution.

Airy disk sets blur size Bigger D → better resolution Shorter λ → better resolution

First minimum angle

θmin ≈ 1.22 λ/D

D is aperture diameter; θ is in radians for small angles.

36.3.1 Airy Disk and Resolution (Rayleigh Criterion)

Rayleigh criterion

θR ≈ 1.22 λ/D
Worked Example: Telescope resolution

Given: D = 0.10 m, λ = 550 nm.

  1. θR = 1.22λ/D = 1.22(5.50×10−7)/0.10 = 6.7×10−6 rad.
  2. In arcseconds: 6.7×10−6 rad × (206265 arcsec/rad) ≈ 1.4 arcsec.

Answer: θR ≈ 6.7×10−6 rad (≈ 1.4 arcsec).

36.4 Diffraction by a Double Slit

Key ideas

With finite-width slits, the two-slit interference pattern is modulated by a single-slit diffraction envelope. You get a set of bright interference fringes whose overall brightness varies with angle.

36.4.1 Interference with a Diffraction Envelope

Interference maxima (slit separation d)

d sinθ = mλ

Envelope minima (slit width a)

a sinθ = mλ

Some interference maxima can be “missing” if they occur at angles where the single-slit envelope has a minimum.

36.5 Diffraction Gratings

Key ideas

A grating has many equally spaced slits. Interference from many slits produces very sharp bright maxima at specific angles, making gratings excellent for separating wavelengths (spectroscopy).

Grating equation

d sinθ = mλ

d is grating spacing (distance between adjacent slits).

36.5.1 Grating Maxima

Allowed orders

|sinθ| ≤ 1 ⇒ mλ ≤ d
Worked Example: Maximum diffraction order

Given: Grating has 600 lines/mm, λ = 500 nm.

  1. d = 1/(600 mm−1) = 1.67×10−3 mm = 1.67×10−6 m.
  2. mmax = ⌊d/λ⌋ = ⌊(1.67×10−6)/(5.00×10−7)⌋ = ⌊3.34⌋ = 3.

Answer: Highest observable order is m = 3.

36.6 Gratings: Dispersion and Resolving Power

Key ideas

Dispersion describes how strongly angle changes with wavelength. Resolving power describes whether two nearby wavelengths can be distinguished as separate lines.

36.6.1 Dispersion

Differentiate the grating equation

d cosθ dθ = m dλ
dθ/dλ = m/(d cosθ)

Higher order m gives larger angular separation for a given Δλ, especially near large θ where cosθ is smaller.

36.6.2 Resolving Power

Rayleigh criterion for gratings

R = λ/Δλ = mN

N is the number of illuminated slits. Larger N produces sharper peaks and better resolution. Increasing the order m also increases resolving power.

36.7 X-Ray Diffraction

Key ideas

X-ray wavelengths are comparable to atomic spacings in crystals. A crystal acts like a 3D diffraction grating: constructive interference occurs for certain angles, revealing lattice spacing.

36.7.1 Bragg’s Law

Constructive interference from crystal planes

2d sinθ = mλ
Worked Example: Lattice spacing from Bragg angle

Given: First order (m = 1), λ = 0.154 nm, Bragg angle θ = 20°.

  1. d = λ/(2 sinθ) = 0.154 nm/(2 sin20°).
  2. sin20° = 0.342 ⇒ d = 0.154/(0.684) nm = 0.225 nm.

Answer: d ≈ 0.225 nm.

Study tip: keep two “diffraction cores” separate: (1) single-slit minima a sinθ = mλ (envelope), (2) many-source interference d sinθ = mλ (two slits or gratings). Most problems are a controlled combination of these.
