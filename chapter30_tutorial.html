30.1 Faraday’s Law and Lenz’s Law

Key ideas

Induction happens when the magnetic flux through a loop changes. The induced emf drives charges around the loop, and the induced current’s magnetic field tends to oppose the change that caused it.

Flux change → emf Minus sign encodes opposition Direction from Lenz + right-hand rule

Strategy: (1) decide how ΦB changes (increasing/decreasing), (2) decide what induced B would oppose that change, (3) use right-hand rule to get current direction, (4) compute emf magnitude from Faraday’s law.

30.1.1 Magnetic Flux

Definition

ΦB = ∫ B · dA

For a uniform field and a flat area: ΦB = BA cosθ, where θ is the angle between B and the area vector. Units: 1 weber (Wb) = 1 T·m2.

30.1.2 Faraday’s Law

Single loop

ℰ = − dΦB/dt

N-turn coil

ℰ = −N dΦB/dt
Worked Example: Changing flux through a coil

Given: N = 200 turns, flux per turn decreases from 5.0 mWb to 1.0 mWb in 0.020 s.

  1. ΔΦB = (1.0 − 5.0) mWb = −4.0×10−3 Wb.
  2. |ℰ| = N |ΔΦB|/Δt = 200(4.0×10−3)/0.020.
  3. |ℰ| = 40 V.

Answer: Induced emf magnitude is 40 V (direction from Lenz’s law).

30.1.3 Lenz’s Law (Direction)

Rule

The induced current produces a magnetic field that opposes the change in the original flux. “Opposes” means: if flux is increasing, the induced field points to reduce it; if flux is decreasing, the induced field points to keep it from dropping.

Direction checklist

Flux Through Loop Induced B Induced Current
ΦB increasing (into page) Out of page Counterclockwise (right-hand rule)
ΦB decreasing (into page) Into page Clockwise

30.2 Induction and Energy Transfers

Key ideas

In “motional emf” setups, a moving conductor in a magnetic field experiences magnetic forces on charges, separating them and producing an emf. If a circuit is closed, the induced current can create a magnetic force that resists motion; the external agent does mechanical work that ends up as thermal energy (Joule heating) in the circuit.

30.2.1 Motional EMF

Rod moving in uniform B

ℰ = Bℓv    (rod length ℓ, speed v, motion ⟂ to B and rod)

A helpful view: charges in the moving rod feel F = q(v × B), which pushes positive and negative charges to opposite ends. The resulting electric field in the rod balances the magnetic force at steady separation.

Worked Example: Induced current in a sliding rod

Given: B = 0.80 T, ℓ = 0.25 m, v = 3.0 m/s, circuit resistance R = 2.0 Ω.

  1. ℰ = Bℓv = (0.80)(0.25)(3.0) = 0.60 V.
  2. i = ℰ/R = 0.60/2.0 = 0.30 A.

Answer: i = 0.30 A (direction from Lenz’s law).

30.2.2 Power and Energy Flow

Mechanical power equals electrical dissipation (steady speed)

Pmech = Fv = iℰ = i2R

The magnetic field does no work on charges (magnetic force is perpendicular to velocity), but it can “mediate” energy transfer by creating an induced current and a resisting force. The work comes from whatever moves the conductor.

30.3 Induced Electric Fields

Key ideas

A changing magnetic field induces a nonconservative electric field. Unlike electrostatic fields, induced E can form closed loops (its field lines are circles/loops).

30.3.1 Faraday’s Law (Field Form)

Integral form

∮ E · ds = − dΦB/dt

The left side is the circulation of the induced electric field around a closed path, even if there is no conducting wire. If a wire loop is present, that circulation is the induced emf around the loop.

30.3.2 Symmetry Example: Circular Induced E

Uniform B changing inside radius R

If a uniform magnetic field through a circular region changes with time, the induced E lines are concentric circles. Choose a circular integration loop and use symmetry so that E has constant magnitude along it.

E(2πr) = − d(πr2B)/dt    (for r ≤ R)
E(2πr) = − d(πR2B)/dt    (for r ≥ R)

30.4 Inductors and Inductance

Key ideas

An inductor is a circuit element designed to have significant inductance. Inductance measures how much magnetic flux linkage you get for a given current.

30.4.1 Inductance Definition

Flux linkage form

L = NΦB/i

Units: henry (H) = Wb/A. Because flux is proportional to current for many geometries (in linear materials), L is a constant.

30.4.2 Solenoid Inductance

Long solenoid (air core)

L = μ0N2A/ℓ
Worked Example: Inductance of a solenoid

Given: N = 500, A = 2.0×10−4 m2, ℓ = 0.20 m.

  1. L = μ0N2A/ℓ = (4π×10−7)(5002)(2.0×10−4)/0.20.
  2. L ≈ 3.1×10−4 H.

Answer: L ≈ 0.31 mH.

30.5 Self-Induction

Key ideas

When the current in a coil changes, the magnetic flux it produces changes too. That changing flux induces an emf in the same coil that tends to oppose the current change (back emf).

30.5.1 Induced EMF in an Inductor

Self-induced emf

ℰL = −L di/dt

Sign meaning: if di/dt > 0 (current increasing), the induced emf is negative relative to the chosen current direction; it acts like a “battery” opposing the rise.

30.6 RL Circuits

Key ideas

An RL circuit responds exponentially to switching because the inductor resists changes in current. The characteristic time is the inductive time constant τL = L/R.

τL = L/R

After 1τ, a rising current reaches about 63% of its final value; a decaying current falls to about 37% of its initial value.

30.6.1 Current Rise (Switch On)

With emf ℰ and resistance R

i(t) = (ℰ/R)(1 − e−t/τL)
Worked Example: Time to reach a target current

Given: ℰ = 12 V, R = 6.0 Ω, L = 3.0 H. Find time to reach i = 1.5 A.

  1. Final current if = ℰ/R = 2.0 A. Time constant τ = L/R = 0.50 s.
  2. i/if = 1 − e−t/τ ⇒ e−t/τ = 1 − 1.5/2.0 = 0.25.
  3. t = −τ ln(0.25) = 0.50(1.386) = 0.69 s.

Answer: t ≈ 0.69 s.

30.6.2 Current Decay (Switch Off)

Exponential decay

i(t) = i0 e−t/τL

When a circuit opens, the inductor tries to keep current flowing. Large induced emf can appear across a gap (sparks) unless a safe path exists (e.g., diode in electronics).

30.7 Energy Stored in a Magnetic Field

Key result

UB = ½ L i2
Worked Example: Energy in an inductor

Given: L = 0.40 H, i = 2.5 A.

  1. UB = ½Li2 = 0.5(0.40)(2.52).
  2. UB = 1.25 J.

Answer: UB = 1.25 J.

30.7.1 Power Into an Inductor

Connection to self-induction

P = iℰL = iL(di/dt)

Integrating power over time from i = 0 to i gives UB = ½Li2. The energy is “in the field” and can be returned to the circuit.

30.8 Energy Density of a Magnetic Field

Energy per unit volume

uB = B2/(2μ0)

This mirrors electric energy density uE = ε0E2/2. It helps estimate energy stored in regions where B is known.

30.8.1 Quick Estimation

Example workflow

  1. Find B in the region (use Ampère’s law / known geometry).
  2. Compute uB = B2/(2μ0).
  3. Multiply by volume V to estimate energy: U ≈ uBV.

30.9 Mutual Induction

Key ideas

Two nearby coils can be coupled: a changing current in coil 1 changes the flux through coil 2, inducing an emf in coil 2. The mutual inductance M depends on geometry and orientation.

30.9.1 Mutual Inductance and Induced EMF

Definition (one common form)

M = N2ΦB2/i1

Induced emf

ℰ2 = −M di1/dt
Worked Example: Induced emf from a changing current

Given: M = 0.12 H, current in coil 1 increases at di1/dt = 4.0 A/s.

  1. |ℰ2| = M|di1/dt| = (0.12)(4.0) = 0.48 V.

Answer: Induced emf magnitude is 0.48 V.

Study tip: always separate magnitude (Faraday’s law) from direction (Lenz’s law + right-hand rules). Draw flux “into/out of page” sketches and decide “increase vs decrease” before doing any algebra.
