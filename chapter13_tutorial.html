13.1 Newton’s Law of Gravitation

Any two masses attract with a force along the line joining them. The law is inverse-square in distance and proportional to both masses.

Inverse-square Central force Always attractive

Gravitational force magnitude

F = Gm1m2/r2

G = 6.67×10−11 N·m2/kg2.

13.2 Gravitation and Superposition

Gravitational forces add as vectors. Net force (or gravitational field) is the vector sum of contributions from each mass.

Gravitational field

g = F/m = GM/r2

Field points toward the mass M.

13.3 Gravitation Near Earth’s Surface

Near Earth, g is approximately constant, but it decreases with altitude because r increases in g = GM/r2.

Altitude dependence (ratio form)

g(h) = g0(R/(R + h))2

R is Earth’s radius; g0 ≈ 9.80 m/s2.

13.4 Gravitation Inside Earth

Inside a spherically symmetric mass distribution, only the mass at radii smaller than your position contributes to the net force (shell theorem). For uniform density, g increases linearly with r.

Uniform-density model

g(r) = g0(r/R)

Real Earth is not uniform, but this model is a useful first estimate.

13.5 Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravitational potential energy is defined so that changes in potential energy equal minus the work done by gravity. For point masses, the zero is taken at infinity.

Two-mass potential energy

U(r) = −GMm/r
ΔU = −Wgrav

13.6 Kepler’s Laws

Planetary motion can be described by Kepler’s empirical laws. Newton’s gravitation explains them and generalizes them to satellites.

Kepler’s third law (circular/elliptic orbit)

T2 ∝ a3
T2 = (4π2/GM)a3

a is the semi-major axis; M is the central mass.

13.7 Satellites: Orbits and Energy

For a circular orbit, gravity supplies the centripetal acceleration. Orbital speed and period depend only on the orbit radius and the central mass.

Circular orbit

v = √(GM/r)
E = −GMm/(2r)
Worked example: low Earth orbit speed

Using v = √(GM/r), with r ≈ R + 400 km. Since GM is fixed for Earth, increasing r slightly reduces v; typical LEO speeds are near 7.7 km/s.

13.8 Einstein and Gravitation

In general relativity, gravity is not a force in the Newtonian sense: mass-energy curves spacetime, and free-falling objects follow geodesics. Newton’s law is an excellent approximation for weak fields and low speeds.

Qualitative takeaway: gravitational time dilation and light bending are real effects, but for most problems in this course, Newtonian gravitation is sufficient.

Study tip: keep three linked ideas together: field (g = GM/r²), potential (U = −GMm/r), and circular-orbit kinematics (v = √(GM/r)). Use whichever makes the calculation easiest.
