44.1 General Properties of Elementary Particles

Key ideas

Particle reactions/decays are constrained by conservation laws: energy, momentum, and certain quantum numbers (like electric charge) must be the same before and after.

Antiparticles exist Quantum numbers constrain reactions Relativity is essential

Relativistic energy–momentum

E2 = (pc)2 + (mc2)2

For a massless particle (photon), m = 0 so E = pc.

44.1.1 Conservation Laws and Quantum Numbers

Common “bookkeeping”

Quantity Symbol Conserved in
Electric charge Q All interactions
Energy, momentum E, p All interactions
Baryon number B Strong, EM, (effectively) weak
Lepton number L Weak (and also strong, EM)
Strangeness S Strong, EM (not weak)

Antiparticles have the same mass but opposite charge and opposite additive quantum numbers (like B and L). For example, an electron has L = +1, a positron has L = −1.

44.1.2 Worked Example: Momentum from Total Energy

Find momentum of an electron with total energy 2.00 MeV

Given: Total energy E = 2.00 MeV. Electron rest energy mec2 = 0.511 MeV.

  1. Use E2 = (pc)2 + (mc2)2 ⇒ pc = √(E2 − (mc2)2).
  2. pc = √((2.00)2 − (0.511)2) MeV = √(4.000 − 0.261) MeV = 1.93 MeV.

Answer: p ≈ 1.93 MeV/c.

44.2 Leptons, Hadrons, and Strangeness

Key ideas

Particles are grouped by how they interact. Leptons (e, μ, τ and neutrinos) do not feel the strong force. Hadrons do feel the strong force and are built from quarks.

44.2.1 Leptons vs Hadrons; Baryons vs Mesons

Classification snapshot

Family Examples Notes
Leptons e, μ, τ, νe, νμ, ντ No strong interaction; neutrinos are neutral and very weakly interacting
Baryons p, n, Λ, Σ, … Hadrons with three quarks; baryon number B = +1
Mesons π, K, … Hadrons with quark + antiquark; baryon number B = 0

Neutrinos make lepton-number bookkeeping possible in beta decay and many particle decays: they carry away energy/momentum “quietly.”

44.2.2 Strangeness and “Fast vs Slow” Decays

Why some hadrons live longer

Strong decays

If a decay can happen via the strong interaction, it is extremely fast and typically conserves strangeness S.

Weak decays

If strangeness must change, the decay proceeds via the weak interaction, which is much slower (longer lifetimes).

Rule of thumb

“Strange” particles (like kaons) can be produced readily in strong interactions, but many of them decay via weak interactions because the strong interaction would forbid changing S.

44.3 Quarks and Messenger Particles

Key ideas

The quark model organizes hadrons: baryons are made of three quarks, mesons are quark–antiquark pairs. Fundamental interactions are described as exchanges of messenger bosons (force carriers).

44.3.1 Quark Charges and Hadron Composition

Charges (common flavors)

u, c, t: +2/3e     d, s, b: −1/3e
Examples: proton and neutron

Proton: uud ⇒ Q = 2/3 + 2/3 − 1/3 = +1. Neutron: udd ⇒ Q = 2/3 − 1/3 − 1/3 = 0.

The strong interaction depends on color charge (not the same as electric charge). Hadrons are color-neutral combinations.

44.3.2 Worked Example: Quark Content from Charge

Determine the quark content of π+ and K+

Given: π+ and K+ are mesons (quark + antiquark) with total charge +1.

  1. Try u (charge +2/3) with an antiquark of charge +1/3. The antiquark with +1/3 is d̄ (since d is −1/3).
  2. Thus π+ = ud̄.
  3. For K+, include strangeness: use u with s̄ (since s is −1/3, so s̄ is +1/3).
  4. Thus K+ = us̄.

Answer: π+ = ud̄, K+ = us̄.

44.4 Cosmology

Key ideas

Modern cosmology describes an expanding universe that began in a hot, dense state. Expansion stretches wavelengths of light (redshift). A major observational pillar is the cosmic microwave background (CMB), relic radiation from early times.

44.4.1 Redshift and Expansion

Definitions

z = (λobs − λemit)/λemit = λobs/λemit − 1
1 + z = anow/aemit

a is the cosmological scale factor: expansion means a grows with time.

Hubble’s law (nearby galaxies)
v ≈ H0d

v is recession speed, d distance, H0 is the Hubble constant.

44.4.2 Worked Example: Redshift and Scale Factor

Find z and anow/aemit from an observed line

Given: A spectral line emitted at λemit = 656 nm is observed at λobs = 984 nm.

  1. z = λobs/λemit − 1 = 984/656 − 1 = 0.50.
  2. Scale-factor ratio: 1 + z = anow/aemit = 1.50.

Answer: z = 0.50 and the universe has expanded by a factor 1.5 since emission.

44.4.3 Worked Example: Age Scale from the Hubble Constant

Estimate the “Hubble time” 1/H0 for H0 = 70 km/s/Mpc

Given: H0 = 70 km/s/Mpc and 1 Mpc = 3.09×1019 km.

  1. Convert: H0 = 70/(3.09×1019) s−1 = 2.27×10−18 s−1.
  2. Hubble time: 1/H0 = 4.41×1017 s.
  3. Convert to years: (4.41×1017 s)/(3.16×107 s/yr) = 1.4×1010 yr.

Answer: 1/H0 ≈ 1.4×1010 years (a useful age scale; modern estimates are of this order).

Study tip: treat particle physics like accounting: list initial and final particles, then check charge, B, L (and S if relevant). For cosmology, convert wavelength shifts into z, then relate z to a(t) via 1 + z.
