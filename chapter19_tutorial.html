19.1 Avogadro’s Number

Key ideas

The mole is a counting unit: 1 mol contains a fixed number of entities (atoms, molecules, etc.). This bridges microscopic masses (kg per molecule) to macroscopic masses (kg per mole).

1 mol = NA particles Connects micro ↔ macro Molar mass matters

Constants

NA = 6.022×1023 mol−1
R = NAk

k is Boltzmann’s constant, 1.38×10−23 J/K; R = 8.314 J/mol·K.

19.1.1 Moles, Molecules, and Mass

Convert molar mass to molecular mass

m (per molecule) = M/NA

Here M is molar mass in kg/mol.

Worked Example: Mass of one N2 molecule

Given: M(N2) = 28.0 g/mol = 2.80×10−2 kg/mol.

  1. m = M/NA = (2.80×10−2)/(6.022×1023) = 4.65×10−26 kg.

Answer: One N2 molecule has mass ≈ 4.7×10−26 kg.

19.2 Ideal Gases

Key ideas

An ideal gas is a model where molecules are point-like, have negligible intermolecular forces except during brief collisions, and the internal energy depends only on temperature. It works well at low density and not-too-low temperature.

Equation of state

PV = nRT

P in Pa, V in m3, T in K, n in mol.

19.2.1 PV = nRT and Simple Scaling

Useful rearrangements

P = (n/V)RT
PV/T = constant (for fixed n)

Always use Kelvin. Temperature differences in °C can be used in ΔT, but absolute thermodynamics uses T in K.

19.3 Pressure, Temperature, and RMS Speed

Key ideas

Kinetic theory links macroscopic pressure to microscopic molecular motion: pressure comes from momentum transfer in collisions with the container walls.

Pressure from collisions Temperature ↔ kinetic energy Typical speed grows with √T

RMS speed

vrms = √(3kT/m) = √(3RT/M)

m is mass per molecule; M is molar mass (kg/mol).

19.3.1 vrms from Temperature and Molar Mass

Interpretation

Lighter gases move faster at the same temperature. Doubling temperature increases vrms by √2, not by 2.

19.3.2 Worked Example: vrms for N2 at 300 K

Compute vrms

Given: T = 300 K, molar mass M = 28.0 g/mol = 2.80×10−2 kg/mol.

  1. Use vrms = √(3RT/M).
  2. vrms = √(3·8.314·300 / 0.0280) = 5.17×102 m/s.

Answer: vrms ≈ 5.2×102 m/s.

19.4 Translational Kinetic Energy

Key ideas

Temperature is a measure of average translational kinetic energy per molecule for an ideal gas. This is why “hotter” means “faster microscopic motion.”

19.4.1 ⟨K⟩ = (3/2)kT and Per-mole Form

Average kinetic energy

⟨K⟩ = (3/2)kT
Per mole: ⟨K⟩mol = (3/2)RT
Worked Example: Average translational energy at 300 K

Given: T = 300 K.

  1. Per mole: (3/2)RT = 1.5·8.314·300 = 3.74×103 J/mol.
  2. Per molecule: (3/2)kT = (3.74×103 J/mol)/NA = 6.21×10−21 J.

Answer: About 3.7 kJ/mol (or 6×10−21 J per molecule) at 300 K.

19.5 Mean Free Path

Key ideas

The mean free path λ is the average distance a molecule travels between collisions. At low pressure, gases are “more rarefied,” collisions are less frequent, and λ increases.

19.5.1 Worked Example: Mean Free Path in Air

Estimate λ at 1 atm, 300 K

Given: Molecular diameter d ≈ 3.7×10−10 m, pressure P = 1.01×105 Pa, temperature T = 300 K.

  1. Use λ = kT/(√2 πd2P).
  2. λ = (1.38×10−23·300)/(1.414·π·(3.7×10−10)2·1.01×105) = 6.7×10−8 m.

Answer: λ ≈ 7×10−8 m = 70 nm at room conditions (order-of-magnitude).

19.6 The Distribution of Molecular Speeds

Key ideas

Not all molecules have the same speed. At a given temperature, speeds follow the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution: many molecules have moderate speeds, few are very slow, and there’s a long high-speed tail.

19.6.1 Most Probable vs Average vs RMS Speed

Three “typical” speeds

vp = √(2RT/M),   v̄ = √(8RT/(πM)),   vrms = √(3RT/M)
Worked Example: Compare speeds for N2 at 300 K

Given: T = 300 K, M = 0.028 kg/mol.

  1. vp = √(2RT/M) = 4.22×102 m/s.
  2. v̄ = √(8RT/(πM)) = 4.76×102 m/s.
  3. vrms = √(3RT/M) = 5.17×102 m/s.

Answer: vp < v̄ < vrms, as expected.

19.7 The Molar Specific Heats of an Ideal Gas

Key ideas

The molar heat capacities tell how much heat is needed to raise temperature under different constraints: constant volume (CV) and constant pressure (CP). For an ideal gas, they differ by R.

19.7.1 CV, CP, and Mayer’s Relation

Definitions

CV = (1/n)(dQ/dT)V,   CP = (1/n)(dQ/dT)P
Mayer’s relation (ideal gas): CP − CV = R

At constant pressure, heating causes the gas to expand and do work (PdV), so more heat is required than at constant volume.

19.8 Degrees of Freedom and Molar Specific Heats

Key ideas

Equipartition says each quadratic degree of freedom contributes (1/2)kT per molecule to average energy. The number of active degrees of freedom depends on temperature and molecular structure.

19.8.1 Equipartition and γ

Heat capacities in terms of degrees of freedom f

CV = (f/2)R,   CP = CV + R = (f/2 + 1)R
γ = CP/CV = (f + 2)/f
Gas type (typical) f γ
Monatomic (He, Ne) 3 5/3 ≈ 1.67
Diatomic (N2, O2 at room T) 5 7/5 = 1.40
Nonlinear polyatomic (often) 6 4/3 ≈ 1.33

19.9 The Adiabatic Expansion of an Ideal Gas

Key ideas

In an adiabatic process, no heat is exchanged: Q = 0. If a gas expands adiabatically, it does work on the surroundings, so its internal energy drops and temperature decreases.

19.9.1 Adiabatic Relations

Useful forms

PVγ = constant
TVγ−1 = constant
TP(1−γ)/γ = constant

γ = CP/CV.

19.9.2 Worked Example: Temperature Drop on Expansion

Find T after adiabatic doubling of volume (diatomic gas)

Given: Ti = 300 K, Vf = 2Vi, diatomic gas so γ = 1.40.

  1. Use TVγ−1 = constant ⇒ Tf = Ti(Vi/Vf)γ−1.
  2. Tf = 300(1/2)0.40 = 2.27×102 K.

Answer: T drops to about 227 K for this ideal adiabatic expansion.

Study tip: when stuck, write the “translation table” between microscopic and macroscopic: k ↔ R, m ↔ M, per-molecule ↔ per-mole. Then pick the right process constraint (isothermal, isochoric, isobaric, adiabatic) and use the matching relation.
