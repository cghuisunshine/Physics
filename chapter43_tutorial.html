43.1 Nuclear Fission

Key ideas

In fission, a heavy nucleus splits into two medium-mass fragments plus a few neutrons. The products have higher binding energy per nucleon, so mass decreases and energy is released.

Heavy → medium fragments Neutrons released ~200 MeV per fission

Energy bookkeeping

Q = (minitial − mfinal)c2

Most of Q becomes kinetic energy of fragments (and some in gammas and neutrinos depending on chain products).

43.1.1 Q-value and Energy Scale

Rule of thumb

A typical U-235 fission releases about 200 MeV. Converting units is useful: 200 MeV ≈ 200×106 eV ≈ 3.2×10−11 J per fission.

The energy per reaction is huge compared with chemical bonds (~eV), but nuclear fuels have to be managed because of radiation and decay heat.

43.1.2 Worked Example: Energy from 1 g of U-235

Estimate energy if all nuclei fission

Given: Energy per fission ≈ 200 MeV = 3.2×10−11 J, molar mass ≈ 235 g/mol.

  1. Number of nuclei in 1 g: N = (1 g / 235 g/mol)NA = (1/235)(6.02×1023) = 2.56×1021.
  2. Total energy: E = N(3.2×10−11 J) = 8.2×1010 J/kg × 0.001 kg = 8.2×107 J.
  3. Convert: 8.2×107 J / (3.6×106 J/kWh) = 22.8 kWh.

Answer: 1 g of U-235 yields about 8×107 J ≈ 23 kWh if fully fissioned.

43.2 The Nuclear Reactor

Key ideas

A reactor sustains a controlled chain reaction to produce steady thermal power. Control is about managing neutrons: slow them (moderator), absorb them (control rods), and keep the system near critical.

43.2.1 Multiplication Factor k and Criticality

Chain reaction condition

k = (number of neutrons in one generation)/(number in previous generation)
State k value What happens
Subcritical k < 1 Reaction dies out
Critical k = 1 Steady power (ideal)
Supercritical k > 1 Neutron population grows

Reactor design aims for k ≈ 1 with feedback mechanisms. In many systems, heating changes material density and moderating efficiency, providing negative feedback that helps stability.

43.2.2 Moderator, Control Rods, Coolant

Core components

Component Job Typical materials
Fuel Fissionable nuclei produce energy and neutrons U-235, Pu-239 (in fuel rods/pellets)
Moderator Slows neutrons to increase fission probability Water, heavy water, graphite
Control rods Absorb neutrons to adjust k Boron, cadmium
Coolant Removes heat from core for power generation Water, CO2, liquid metal

“Decay heat” persists after shutdown because fission products remain radioactive. Cooling and heat removal remain essential even when k < 1.

43.3 A Natural Nuclear Reactor

Key ideas

The Oklo site (Gabon) provides evidence that natural fission reactors operated about 2 billion years ago. Conditions aligned: enough U-235 in natural uranium at that time, water as a moderator, and geologic containment.

43.3.1 Oklo: Why Nature Could Run Fission

Conditions (conceptual checklist)

Requirement Why it matters
Sufficient U-235 fraction Higher probability of sustaining a chain reaction
Moderator present (water) Thermal neutrons promote fission in U-235
Geometry/containment Enough neutrons stay in the region (k not too small)
Self-regulation Boiling water reduces moderation, lowering k (negative feedback)

Oklo is a remarkable real-world example of criticality and moderation: the same physics as reactors, but controlled by geology and water flow.

43.4 Thermonuclear Fusion: The Basic Process

Key ideas

In fusion, light nuclei combine into heavier nuclei with greater binding energy per nucleon (for small A), releasing energy. Fusion requires nuclei to get close despite Coulomb repulsion.

Light → heavier (up to iron) Needs high T and confinement Quantum tunneling helps

Typical fusion fuel

D + T → He-4 + n + 17.6 MeV

D is deuterium (H-2), T is tritium (H-3).

43.4.1 D-T Fusion and Coulomb Barrier

Why the temperature is enormous

To fuse, nuclei must approach within ~10−15 m so the strong force can bind them. Classically, Coulomb repulsion would require huge kinetic energy; in practice, fusion rates rely on the high-energy tail of thermal motion plus quantum tunneling.

“Thermonuclear” means temperature-driven: heating increases collision energy and collision frequency, boosting fusion probability.

43.4.2 Worked Example: Energy per Mole of D-T Reactions

Convert 17.6 MeV per reaction into joules and compare scales

Given: D-T energy release 17.6 MeV, 1 eV = 1.60×10−19 J, NA = 6.02×1023.

  1. Per reaction: E = (17.6×106 eV)(1.60×10−19 J/eV) = 2.82×10−12 J.
  2. Per mole of reactions: Emol = NAE = (6.02×1023)(2.82×10−12) = 1.70×1012 J.
  3. Convert: 1.70×1012 J / (3.6×106 J/kWh) = 4.7×105 kWh.

Answer: One mole of D-T reactions releases ~1.7×1012 J (~4.7×105 kWh).

43.5 Thermonuclear Fusion in the Sun and Other Stars

Key ideas

Stars are long-lived fusion reactors. Gravity provides confinement; high core temperature sustains fusion. The dominant pathway depends on temperature and composition: the Sun primarily uses the proton-proton chain, while more massive stars rely more on the CNO cycle.

43.5.1 Proton-Proton Chain (Overview)

Qualitative steps

What happens Why it’s slow
Protons ultimately combine into He-4 First step requires the weak interaction (p → n), which is rare
Energy emerges as photons + neutrinos Neutrinos escape quickly; photons random-walk outward for a long time

The slowness of the weak-interaction step is a “feature”: it prevents the Sun from burning out quickly.

43.6 Controlled Thermonuclear Fusion

Key ideas

Controlled fusion must (1) heat fuel to very high temperature, (2) keep it dense enough, and (3) confine it long enough for reactions to occur. Different strategies try to satisfy the “triple product” requirements in different ways.

43.6.1 Magnetic Confinement (Tokamak Idea)

Plasma confinement concept

Charged particles spiral along magnetic field lines. A tokamak uses a strong toroidal field (around the donut) plus a poloidal component (twist) so particles stay confined away from the walls. Heating methods include ohmic heating, neutral beam injection, and RF heating.

The hard part is stability and confinement time: turbulence and instabilities let energy leak out faster than fusion can replenish it.

43.6.2 Inertial Confinement and Ignition Idea

Compression instead of long-time confinement

In inertial confinement, lasers (or ion beams) compress and heat a tiny fuel pellet so rapidly that fusion occurs before the pellet can fly apart. Confinement time is extremely short, but density is extremely high.

What “ignition” means (conceptual)

Ignition occurs when alpha particles from fusion deposit enough energy into the plasma to sustain further fusion without external heating (net self-heating dominates losses).

Study tip: in fission problems, track neutrons and k; in fusion problems, track temperature, density, and confinement time. In both, use Q = Δmc2 and practice unit conversions between eV, J, and kWh.
