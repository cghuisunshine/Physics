9.1 Center of Mass

The center of mass (CM) is the “balance point” of a mass distribution. For motion problems, the CM behaves as if all mass were concentrated there, as long as you account only for external forces.

Definition (discrete masses)

rcm = (1/M)∑ mi ri

In components: xcm = (1/M)∑mixi, etc.

9.2 Newton’s Second Law for a System of Particles

Internal forces cancel in pairs when summing over a system, so the net external force controls the system’s total momentum and the CM acceleration.

System form

Fext = dP/dt
Fext = M acm

P is the total momentum of the system.

9.3 Linear Momentum

Momentum is the quantity conserved in collisions and explosions when external forces are negligible. It is the natural quantity tied to Newton’s second law.

p = mv
P = ∑ pi

9.4 Collision and Impulse

Impulse measures the effect of a force over time. In a collision, large forces act for a short time, and impulse captures the net momentum change.

Impulse–momentum theorem

J = ∫ F dt = Δp
F̄Δt = Δp

9.5 Conservation of Linear Momentum

If the net external impulse is zero (or negligible), total momentum is conserved. This is the main tool for explosions and collisions.

Jext = 0 ⇒ Pinitial = Pfinal

Momentum conservation does not require energy conservation (inelastic collisions lose kinetic energy).

9.6 Momentum and Kinetic Energy in Collisions

Collisions are classified by whether kinetic energy is conserved. Momentum is conserved (approximately) whenever external impulse is negligible, but kinetic energy is only conserved in elastic collisions.

Type Momentum Kinetic energy
Elastic Conserved Conserved
Inelastic Conserved Decreases
Perfectly inelastic Conserved Decreases; objects stick

9.7 Elastic Collisions in One Dimension

In 1D elastic collisions, you can solve using momentum conservation plus kinetic-energy conservation (or an equivalent relative-speed relation).

Two equations (1D, elastic)

m1v1i + m2v2i = m1v1f + m2v2f
(1/2)m1v1i2 + (1/2)m2v2i2 = (1/2)m1v1f2 + (1/2)m2v2f2

9.8 Collisions in Two Dimensions

In 2D, conserve momentum in x and y separately. For many problems, energy conservation is not available (inelastic), so geometry and components do the heavy lifting.

Component form

Px,i = Px,f   and   Py,i = Py,f

9.9 Systems with Varying Mass: A Rocket

For variable-mass systems, you must track momentum carried away by expelled mass. The key idea: thrust arises from momentum flux.

Useful result (ideal rocket, no external forces)

Δv = ve ln(m0/m)

ve is exhaust speed relative to the rocket; m decreases from m0 to m.

Study tip: for collisions, decide first what is conserved (momentum always if isolated; kinetic energy only if elastic). Draw “before/after” diagrams and use components.
