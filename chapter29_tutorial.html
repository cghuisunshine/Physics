29.1 Magnetic Field Due to a Current

Key ideas

Moving charge produces magnetic field. For steady currents, we compute B by summing contributions from small current elements. The magnetic field direction wraps around the current (right-hand rules).

Superposition applies Current element is a vector Direction via cross products

29.1.1 Biot–Savart Law

Vector form

dB = (μ0/4π) (i ds × r)/r2

Magnitude form

dB = (μ0/4π) (i ds sinθ)/r2

θ is the angle between the current element direction and the line from the element to the field point. If the point lies along the element direction (θ = 0 or 180°), then dB = 0.

29.1.2 Long Straight Wire

Result

B = μ0i/(2πR)    (perpendicular distance R)

Direction

Field lines are circles centered on the wire. Curl fingers of your right hand with your thumb along the current; your fingers show the direction of B.

Worked Example: Field magnitude near a wire

Given: i = 10 A, R = 2.0 cm.

  1. B = μ0i/(2πR) = (4π×10−7)(10)/(2π·0.020).
  2. B = 1.0 × 10−4 T = 0.10 mT.

Answer: B = 1.0×10−4 T.

29.1.3 Circular Arcs and Loops

Arc at its center

B = (μ0iϕ)/(4πR)    (arc radius R, subtended angle ϕ in radians)

Full circular loop (special case ϕ = 2π)

B = μ0i/(2R)    (at center of a single circular loop)
Worked Example: Center field of a loop

Given: i = 3.0 A, R = 5.0 cm.

  1. B = μ0i/(2R) = (4π×10−7)(3.0)/(2·0.050).
  2. B ≈ 3.77 × 10−5 T.

Answer: B ≈ 3.8×10−5 T.

29.2 Force Between Two Parallel Currents

Key ideas

A current-carrying wire sits in the magnetic field created by the other wire and experiences a magnetic force. This is the origin of attraction/repulsion between currents and historically underpins the SI ampere definition.

29.2.1 Attraction vs. Repulsion

Rules

Parallel currents attract (same direction).

Antiparallel currents repel (opposite directions).

29.2.2 Force per Unit Length

Result

F/L = (μ0i1i2)/(2πd)
Worked Example: Force per length between wires

Given: i1 = 20 A, i2 = 30 A, separation d = 5.0 cm.

  1. F/L = (μ0i1i2)/(2πd).
  2. F/L = (4π×10−7)(600)/(2π·0.050) = 2.4×10−3 N/m.

Answer: 2.4 mN per meter (attractive if currents are parallel).

29.3 Ampère’s Law

Key idea

Ampère’s law is the magnetic analogue of Gauss’s law: it relates the circulation of B around a closed loop to the net current passing through the loop. It is most powerful when symmetry makes B constant along the chosen loop.

Integral form

∮ B · ds = μ0 ienc

29.3.1 Amperian Loops and Sign Convention

Key ideas

Choose a traversal direction for the loop. Curl the fingers of your right hand in that direction; your thumb gives the positive direction for currents through the surface. Currents opposite the thumb count as negative in ienc.

29.3.2 Long Straight Wire (Inside/Outside)

Outside the wire (radius r ≥ a)

B(r) = μ0i/(2πr)

Inside a solid wire (uniform current density, r < a)

B(r) = μ0i r/(2πa2)
Worked Example: Field at the surface vs. inside

Given: wire radius a = 2.0 mm carrying i = 5.0 A. Compare B at r = a and at r = a/2.

  1. At r = a: B = μ0i/(2πa).
  2. At r = a/2 (inside): B = μ0i(a/2)/(2πa2) = (1/2)·μ0i/(2πa).

Answer: B(a/2) is half of B(a) for uniform current density.

29.4 Solenoids and Toroids

Key ideas

A solenoid and a toroid are practical geometries where Ampère’s law gives simple field results. Solenoids produce nearly uniform fields inside; toroids confine fields largely to their core.

29.4.1 Solenoid Field

Ideal long solenoid (away from ends)

B = μ0 n i

n is turns per unit length (N/L).

Worked Example: Field inside a solenoid

Given: n = 1200 turns/m, i = 0.80 A.

  1. B = μ0ni = (4π×10−7)(1200)(0.80) ≈ 1.21×10−3 T.

Answer: B ≈ 1.2 mT.

29.4.2 Toroid Field

Inside the toroid core

B(r) = μ0 i N/(2πr)

r is distance from the toroid center to the point; N is total turns.

Unlike an ideal solenoid’s interior field, a toroid’s B varies with r (stronger on the inner side).

29.5 A Current-Carrying Coil as a Magnetic Dipole

Key ideas

A circular coil behaves like a magnetic dipole: it has a dipole moment μ and produces a dipole-like field far from the coil. On the axis, the field can be computed exactly by Biot–Savart.

29.5.1 Dipole Moment of a Coil

Definition

μ = NiA

Direction: right-hand rule (thumb gives μ for current curl).

29.5.2 Field on the Axis (Single Circular Loop)

Exact axial field

B(z) = μ0 i R2 / (2(R2 + z2)3/2)
Worked Example: Field at the center (z = 0)

Given: Use the axial formula with z = 0.

  1. B(0) = μ0iR2 / (2R3) = μ0i/(2R).

Answer: It reduces to the standard loop-center field.

29.5.3 Far-Field Dipole Form (z ≫ R)

Axial far field

B(z) = (μ0/2π) μ/z3

Don’t confuse the magnetic dipole moment μ with the permeability constant μ0.

Study tip: For B from currents, pick the tool that matches symmetry: Biot–Savart for arbitrary geometry; Ampère’s law for highly symmetric cases (straight wire, solenoid, toroid).
