40.1 Properties of Atoms

Key ideas

Atoms have characteristic sizes (~Å), characteristic binding energies (~eV), and characteristic photons they absorb/emit when electrons transition between allowed states.

Discrete electron energies Photons carry ΔE Spectra fingerprint elements

Photon energy

E = hf = hc/λ

Spectral lines correspond to specific λ (or f) because energy differences are quantized.

40.1.1 Spectral Lines as Energy Differences

Emission/absorption

A transition from a higher energy level to a lower one emits a photon with energy hf = Einitial − Efinal. Absorption reverses the direction.

A useful shortcut is hc ≈ 1240 eV·nm. Then λ in nm is easy if ΔE is in eV.

40.2 The Stern–Gerlach Experiment

Key ideas

A beam of atoms passing through a nonuniform magnetic field splits into discrete spots, revealing that magnetic moments (and angular momentum components) are quantized, not continuous.

40.2.1 Spin-1/2 and Space Quantization

Two-valued outcome

For an electron’s intrinsic angular momentum (spin), measuring a component (say along z) gives only two values: “spin up” or “spin down.” That’s why a silver-atom beam splits into two.

Energy in a field (idea)

U = −μ·B

In a field gradient, different spin states feel different forces and separate spatially.

40.3 Magnetic Resonance

Key ideas

A magnetic moment in a magnetic field precesses like a top. If you apply an oscillating magnetic field at the right frequency, the system absorbs energy strongly: resonance. This underlies NMR and MRI.

40.3.1 Larmor Precession and Resonance Condition

Resonance frequency

ω0 = γB, f0 = ω0/(2π) = (γ/2π)B

γ is the gyromagnetic ratio (depends on particle: electron, proton, etc.).

In NMR, you often see (γ/2π) reported directly in MHz/T. For protons: (γ/2π) ≈ 42.58 MHz/T.

40.3.2 Worked Example: Proton Resonance Frequency

Find the NMR frequency for protons in a 1.5 T magnet

Given: (γ/2π) = 42.58 MHz/T for a proton, magnetic field B = 1.5 T.

  1. f0 = (γ/2π)B = (42.58 MHz/T)(1.5 T) = 63.9 MHz.

Answer: f0 ≈ 64 MHz.

40.4 Exclusion Principle and Multiple Electrons in a Trap

Key ideas

Electrons are fermions. The Pauli exclusion principle says no two electrons in the same atom can share the same complete set of quantum numbers. This single rule builds most of the structure of atoms and chemistry.

40.4.1 Pauli Principle and “Two per Orbital”

Practical takeaway

For a given spatial orbital (same n, ℓ, mℓ), there are two allowed spin states. So each orbital holds at most two electrons (spin up and spin down).

Shell capacity (result)

max electrons in shell n = 2n2

Because there are n2 spatial orbitals for a given n and 2 spin states each.

40.5 Building the Periodic Table

Key ideas

The periodic table is a map of how electrons fill available atomic states. As protons are added (higher Z), electrons occupy orbitals in an order shaped by energy, screening, and the exclusion principle.

40.5.1 Quantum Numbers and Shell Capacity

Quantum numbers (quick map)

Label Symbol Typical allowed values Meaning (short)
Principal n 1, 2, 3, … Shell (overall size/energy scale)
Orbital ℓ 0 … (n − 1) Subshell (s, p, d, f …)
Magnetic mℓ −ℓ … +ℓ Orbital orientation index
Spin ms ±1/2 Two-valued intrinsic angular momentum
Quick check: how many electrons fit in n = 3?

Use: 2n2 = 2·32 = 18.

Answer: The n = 3 shell holds up to 18 electrons.

40.5.2 Periodic Trends (Why Patterns Repeat)

Idea

Chemical properties are governed by valence electrons (outermost partially filled shell/subshell). When a shell is filled, the next element begins filling a new shell, and the pattern repeats across a period.

Many trends (atomic radius, ionization energy) come from a tug-of-war between increasing nuclear charge Z and electron screening by inner shells.

40.6 X Rays and the Ordering of the Elements

Key ideas

X rays are produced when energetic electrons strike a target: a continuum (“bremsstrahlung”) plus sharp characteristic lines from atomic inner-shell transitions. The characteristic frequencies correlate strongly with atomic number Z.

40.6.1 X-ray Continuum + Characteristic Lines

Two pieces of an x-ray spectrum

Continuum (cutoff)

The shortest possible wavelength occurs if an electron’s entire kinetic energy (from voltage V) becomes one photon.

λmin = hc/(eV)

Characteristic lines

If an inner-shell electron is knocked out, a higher electron drops down and emits a photon with a specific energy. These lines are element-dependent (fingerprints).

40.6.2 Worked Example: Cutoff Wavelength

Find the minimum wavelength from a 50 kV x-ray tube

Given: Tube voltage V = 50 kV, and hc ≈ 1240 eV·nm.

  1. Electron energy: eV = 50,000 eV.
  2. λmin = hc/(eV) = (1240 eV·nm)/(50,000 eV) = 0.0248 nm.

Answer: λmin ≈ 2.5×10−2 nm.

40.7 Lasers

Key ideas

A laser amplifies light by stimulated emission, producing a coherent beam (same frequency and phase relation). Achieving amplification requires a population inversion plus an optical cavity for feedback.

40.7.1 Stimulated Emission and Gain

Three processes

Process What happens Photon output
Absorption Atom absorbs a photon and moves up in energy Photon removed
Spontaneous emission Atom drops and emits a photon randomly Random direction/phase
Stimulated emission Incoming photon triggers emission of a second photon Second photon matches the first

Without population inversion (more atoms in the upper level than the lower), absorption dominates and you cannot get net gain.

40.7.2 Three-Level Laser Idea

How inversion is engineered

A pump excites atoms from the ground state to a high state that quickly decays into a long-lived metastable level. Because the metastable level accumulates population, it becomes possible to achieve inversion between that level and a lower level, enabling stimulated emission in the cavity.

Study tip: keep the “two quantizations” distinct: (1) energy levels (spectra) and (2) angular momentum components (Stern–Gerlach). Then connect them to tools: resonance probes energy splitting, x rays probe inner shells, and lasers exploit stimulated emission.
