41.1 The Electrical Properties of Metals

Key ideas

In a metal, many electrons are available to move through the lattice. An applied electric field nudges their motion, producing a small net drift velocity superimposed on rapid thermal motion.

Lots of mobile carriers Small drift speeds Collisions set resistance

Ohmic form

J = σE

Current density J is proportional to electric field E. Conductivity σ is large for good metals.

41.1.1 Drift, Current Density, Conductivity

Useful relations

I = nqAvd
J = nqvd
σ = nqμ = nq2τ/m

μ is mobility, τ is mean time between collisions (“relaxation time”). Bigger τ means carriers keep their drift longer, so σ is larger.

Why does resistivity increase with temperature in metals?

Higher temperature increases lattice vibration (phonons), which increases scattering and decreases τ. Since σ ∝ τ, conductivity drops, and resistivity ρ = 1/σ rises.

41.1.2 Worked Example: Drift Speed in Copper

Estimate electron drift speed in a wire

Given: Copper wire carries I = 2.0 A, cross-section A = 1.0 mm2 = 1.0×10−6 m2. Take carrier density n ≈ 8.5×1028 m−3 (≈ 1 conduction electron per atom).

  1. Use I = nqAvd ⇒ vd = I/(nqA).
  2. vd = 2.0 / (8.5×1028 · 1.60×10−19 · 1.0×10−6) = 1.47×10−4 m/s.

Answer: vd ≈ 1.5×10−4 m/s ≈ 0.15 mm/s (surprisingly slow).

41.2 Semiconductors and Doping

Key ideas

Semiconductors have far fewer free carriers than metals at room temperature. Conduction can occur by electrons in the conduction band and by holes in the valence band. Adding small amounts of impurity atoms (doping) can increase carrier density dramatically.

41.2.1 Carriers: Electrons and Holes

Conductivity (two-carrier form)

σ = q(nμn + pμp)

n is electron density, p is hole density, μ are mobilities.

n-type

Donor impurities add electrons: n increases (electrons dominate).

p-type

Acceptor impurities create holes: p increases (holes dominate).

“Holes” behave like positive-charge carriers: they move opposite electron drift in the valence band picture, but they produce current in the same direction as a positive charge would.

41.2.2 Worked Example: Doped Silicon Conductivity

Estimate σ for n-type Si with ND = 1016 cm−3

Given: Assume n ≈ ND = 1016 cm−3, electron mobility μn ≈ 1350 cm2/(V·s), and ignore the hole term. Use q = 1.60×10−19 C.

  1. σ ≈ qnμn = (1.60×10−19)(1016)(1350) = 2.16 (Ω·cm)−1.
  2. Resistivity ρ = 1/σ ≈ 0.46 Ω·cm.

Answer: Doping to 1016 cm−3 yields σ ≈ 2.2 (Ω·cm)−1 (ρ ≈ 0.46 Ω·cm).

41.3 The p-n Junction and the Transistor

Key ideas

When p-type and n-type regions touch, carriers diffuse and leave behind charged ions. The result is a depletion region with an internal electric field. This built-in field makes current flow easy in one bias direction and difficult in the other: a diode.

41.3.1 Depletion Region and Diode Behavior

Bias directions

Forward bias

Apply a voltage that reduces the barrier. The depletion region shrinks; carriers cross and recombine, producing a large current.

Reverse bias

Apply a voltage that increases the barrier. The depletion region widens; only a tiny leakage current flows (until breakdown).

Diode I–V shape (qualitative)

The current rises very rapidly with forward voltage and remains near zero for moderate reverse voltage. A useful scale is the thermal voltage VT = kT/q ≈ 25.9 mV at room temperature, which appears in exponential diode models.

41.3.2 Transistor as a Controlled Current Source

Big idea

A transistor uses p-n junction physics to let a small input signal control a much larger current. In a bipolar junction transistor (BJT), a small base current (or base–emitter voltage) controls the collector current. In field-effect transistors (FETs), a gate voltage controls a conducting channel.

Circuit viewpoint: the transistor is an active element that can provide amplification because it controls how much current a power source delivers to a load.

Study tip: for conduction problems, write the same story three ways: microscopic (n, μ, τ), field form (J = σE), and circuit form (V = IR). Then decide which variables a problem gives you and which story matches.
