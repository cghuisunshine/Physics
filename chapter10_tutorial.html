10.1 Rotational Variables

Rotation uses angular analogs of linear variables: angle θ, angular velocity ω, and angular acceleration α. The kinematics parallels 1D motion.

Definitions

ω = dθ/dt
α = dω/dt

10.2 Rotation with Constant Angular Acceleration

With constant α, the same “constant acceleration” equations apply with θ ↔ x, ω ↔ v, and α ↔ a.

Kinematics (constant α)

ω = ω0 + αt
θ − θ0 = ω0t + (1/2)αt2
ω2 = ω02 + 2α(θ − θ0)

10.3 Relating the Linear and Angular Variables

Points on a rigid rotating body have linear speed proportional to radius: larger radius moves faster.

Rigid-body relations

v = ωr
at = αr
ar = ω2r = v2/r

10.4 Kinetic Energy of Rotation

Rotation stores kinetic energy depending on how mass is distributed about the rotation axis (rotational inertia).

Krot = (1/2)Iω2

10.5 Calculating the Rotational Inertia

Rotational inertia is I = Σmr2 (discrete) or I = ∫r2dm (continuous). The parallel-axis theorem shifts I to a different axis.

Definitions

I = ∑ miri2
I = ∫ r2 dm
I = Icm + Md2 (parallel-axis)

10.6 Torque

Torque is the rotational “push” produced by a force at a lever arm. Perpendicular lever arm matters most.

τ = rF sin θ

10.7 Newton’s Second Law for Rotation

For rotation about a fixed axis, net torque produces angular acceleration proportional to I.

τnet = Iα
Worked example: angular acceleration from torque

If τ = 6.0 N·m acts on a wheel with I = 0.50 kg·m2, then α = τ/I = 12 rad/s2.

10.8 Work and Rotational Kinetic Energy

Work in rotation parallels linear work: torque does work through angular displacement, changing rotational kinetic energy.

Work–energy

W = ∫ τ dθ
W = ΔKrot = (1/2)I(ωf2 − ωi2)
P = τω

Study tip: translate problems into the rotational analogs: x→θ, v→ω, a→α, F→τ, m→I, and K=(1/2)mv²→(1/2)Iω².
