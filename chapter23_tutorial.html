23.1 Electric Flux

Key ideas

Electric flux Φ measures how much electric field “pierces” a surface (field lines crossing the surface), not how much skims along it.

Flux depends on field direction + surface orientation Use a dot product: piercing component only Net flux is defined for closed (Gaussian) surfaces

Important formulas & meaning

dΦ = E · dA
Φ = ∫ E · dA (total flux through a surface)
Units of flux: N·m2/C. For a closed surface, use the closed-surface integral symbol: ∮.
Worked Example: Uniform field through a tilted flat surface

Given: Uniform field E = 300 N/C. A flat surface has area A = 0.020 m2. The area vector makes angle θ = 60° with E.

Find: Flux through the surface.

  1. For a flat surface in uniform field, Φ = E · A = EA cosθ.
  2. Compute: Φ = (300)(0.020)cos60° = 6.0(0.50) = 3.0 N·m2/C.

Answer: Φ = +3.0 N·m2/C (positive because the field has a component along the area vector).

23.1.1 Flux, Area Vectors, and Signs

Key ideas

Area vector: For a small patch, dA points perpendicular to the surface patch. Its magnitude is the patch area dA.

Closed surface sign rule: For a closed surface, dA is defined as pointing outward. Field entering the surface gives negative contribution; field leaving gives positive.

Dot product shortcut

E · dA = E dA cosθ

θ is the angle between E and the area vector (not the surface itself).

Worked Example: Net flux through a cube in a uniform field

Given: A cube is in a uniform electric field (same magnitude/direction everywhere). No charge is inside the cube.

Find: Net flux Φ through the cube.

  1. Uniform field lines enter some faces and leave opposite faces.
  2. For each pair of opposite faces, the flux contributions cancel (same magnitude, opposite sign).
  3. Therefore the net flux through the closed surface is zero.

Answer: Φ = 0, consistent with Gauss’ law when qenc = 0.

Common mistake: Using the surface’s “tilt angle” directly. Always use the angle between E and the area vector (normal direction).

23.2 Gauss’ Law

Key ideas

Gauss’ law relates the net flux through a closed surface (a Gaussian surface) to the net enclosed charge qenc.

Only enclosed charge matters for net flux Outside charges can distort E, but not net flux Best when symmetry makes E constant on the surface

Important formulas & their meaning

ε0 Φ = qenc
ε0 ∮ E · dA = qenc

If qenc > 0, net flux is outward (positive). If qenc < 0, net flux is inward (negative).

Worked Example: Enclosed charge from measured net flux

Given: Net flux through a closed surface is Φ = +2.4×103 N·m2/C.

Find: Enclosed charge qenc.

  1. Use Gauss’ law: qenc = ε0Φ.
  2. qenc = (8.85×10−12 C2/(N·m2))(2.4×103 N·m2/C)
  3. qenc ≈ 2.1×10−8 C.

Answer: qenc ≈ +2.1×10−8 C (positive because flux is outward).

Checkpoint 23.2.1 (concept): Outside charge and net flux

Question: If you bring a big charge close to (but outside) a Gaussian surface, does that charge change the net flux through the surface?

Answer: No. Outside charges can change the field pattern on the surface, but Gauss’ law says the net flux depends only on qenc.

23.2a How to Use Gauss’ Law (Method)

When Gauss’ law gives you E quickly

Gauss’ law is always true, but it only makes finding E easy when you can choose a Gaussian surface where E is either constant in magnitude and parallel to dA, or else perpendicular to dA (zero dot product) on parts of the surface.

Problem-solving checklist

Step What to decide Common choice
1 What symmetry does the charge distribution have? Spherical / cylindrical / planar
2 Pick a Gaussian surface matching that symmetry. Sphere / cylinder / pillbox
3 Evaluate ∮ E · dA by symmetry. E is constant over parts of surface
4 Compute enclosed charge qenc. Point charge / λL / σA / ρV
5 Solve for E and state direction. Use sign of charge + symmetry
If the charge distribution lacks symmetry (or the Gaussian surface cannot be chosen so that E is constant over it), Gauss’ law usually won’t directly give you E; you may need Coulomb’s law/integration methods from Chapter 22.

23.3 A Charged Isolated Conductor

Key ideas

Excess charge lives on the surface: In electrostatic equilibrium, any excess charge on a conductor moves to its outer surface.

Reason: if E existed inside the conductor, charges would keep moving.

Field inside is zero: The electric field inside the conducting material is zero in electrostatic equilibrium.

This is a key “boundary condition” you use with Gaussian surfaces.

Important formulas & their meaning

E = σ / ε0 (just outside a conductor surface)

Direction: perpendicular to the surface; outward if σ > 0 and inward if σ < 0. (Inside the conductor: E = 0.)

Worked Example (Checkpoint-style): Charge in a spherical conducting shell

Given: A spherical metal shell contains a central point charge +Q. The shell has net charge +3Q.

Find: (a) charge on inner surface, (b) charge on outer surface, (c) effective q in E = kq/r2 in different regions.

  1. (a) Inner surface must have charge −Q so that the field inside the conductor material is zero.
  2. (b) Shell’s total is +3Q, so outer surface has +3Q − (−Q) = +4Q.
  3. (c) For r between the point charge and the inner surface, q = +Q (enclosed is just the point charge).
  4. (d) For r within the conductor material (between inner and outer surfaces), E = 0 (do not use kq/r2).
  5. (e) For r outside the outer surface, enclosed net is +Q + 3Q = +4Q, so q = +4Q.

Answer: qinner = −Q, qouter = +4Q; use q = +Q inside cavity, E = 0 in conductor, q = +4Q outside.

On nonspherical conductors, σ is generally not uniform. Sharp curvature tends to concentrate charge (larger σ), making the local field just outside larger.

23.4 Applying Gauss’ Law: Cylindrical Symmetry

Key ideas

For an infinitely long line of charge (or a long uniformly charged rod), the electric field is radial and depends only on the perpendicular distance r from the axis.

Important formulas & their meaning

E = λ / (2π ε0 r) (infinite line of charge)

λ is linear charge density (C/m). Direction: radially outward for λ > 0, inward for λ < 0.

Worked Example: Field near a line of charge

Given: λ = −2.0 μC/m. Find the field magnitude at r = 0.10 m.

Find: E (magnitude + direction).

  1. Use E = |λ|/(2π ε0 r).
  2. E = (2.0×10−6)/(2π(8.85×10−12)(0.10)) ≈ 3.6×105 N/C.
  3. Since λ is negative, the field points radially toward the line.

Answer: E ≈ 3.6×105 N/C, directed inward toward the line.

Optional extension: Infinite solid cylinder with uniform volume charge density

Result (from Gauss’ law): For a cylinder of radius R with uniform ρ, using a coaxial Gaussian cylinder:

E(r<R) = ρr / (2ε0),    E(r>R) = ρR2 / (2ε0r)

Direction is radial outward for ρ > 0 and inward for ρ < 0.

23.5 Applying Gauss’ Law: Planar Symmetry

Key ideas

A large (effectively infinite) charged sheet produces a uniform electric field that is perpendicular to the sheet. A “pillbox” Gaussian surface makes the flux calculation simple.

Important formulas & their meaning

E = σ / (2ε0) (infinite nonconducting sheet)
E = σ / ε0 (just outside a charged conductor surface)

For the sheet, the field has the same magnitude on both sides (opposite directions). For the conductor, the field is zero inside.

Worked Example: Two infinite parallel sheets (superposition)

Given: Two infinite nonconducting sheets are parallel. Sheet A has σA = +6.8 μC/m2, sheet B has σB = −4.3 μC/m2.

Find: Net field magnitude and direction (i) between the sheets, (ii) outside the pair.

  1. Field from one sheet: E = |σ|/(2ε0), directed away from +σ and toward −σ.
  2. Between the sheets, both fields point the same way (from + toward −), so magnitudes add.
  3. Outside the sheets, the fields oppose, so magnitudes subtract.

Answer: Between: E = (|σA|+|σB|)/(2ε0) directed from A to B. Outside: E = (|σA|−|σB|)/(2ε0) with direction set by the larger |σ|.

23.6 Applying Gauss’ Law: Spherical Symmetry

Key ideas

Spherical shell (outside): Field is the same as if all the charge were at the center.

E points radially outward for +q and inward for −q.

Spherical shell (inside): Field due to a uniform spherical shell is zero at any point inside the shell.

This is a powerful symmetry result (a “shell theorem”).

Important formulas & their meaning

E = (1 / (4π ε0)) · (q / r2) (outside a spherical shell)
E = (1 / (4π ε0)) · (q / R3) · r (inside a uniformly charged solid sphere)

In the solid-sphere formula, q is the total charge, R is the sphere radius, and r is the distance from the center (r < R).

Worked Example: Field inside vs outside a uniformly charged solid sphere

Given: A nonconducting sphere has radius R = 0.20 m and total charge q = +6.0 nC uniformly distributed.

Find: E at (a) r = 0.10 m and (b) r = 0.30 m.

  1. (a) Inside (r < R): E = (1/(4π ε0))(q/R3)r. Using k = 8.99×109, E = k q r / R3.
  2. E = (8.99×109)(6.0×10−9)(0.10)/(0.20)3 ≈ 6.7×102 N/C.
  3. (b) Outside (r > R): Treat as point charge: E = kq/r2 = (8.99×109)(6.0×10−9)/(0.30)2 ≈ 6.0×102 N/C.
  4. Direction in both cases: radially outward (q positive).

Answer: E(0.10 m) ≈ 6.7×102 N/C outward; E(0.30 m) ≈ 6.0×102 N/C outward.

Key pattern: for spherical symmetry, Gauss’ law reduces the field problem to “how much charge is enclosed within radius r?” Then E depends only on qenc(r) and r.

Study tip: When using Gauss’ law to find E, always write (1) the symmetry, (2) the Gaussian surface, (3) the flux integral simplification, and (4) qenc. If any of these is unclear, step back and redraw the geometry and field direction.
